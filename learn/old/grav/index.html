Universal Gravitation

We have already studied how the force of gravity works on Earth’s surface. In this topic, we will study gravity more generally to understand how it works within the solar system and the larger universe.

This topic corresponds to General Learning Outcome B2 of the Physics 20 Program of Studies, and to chapter 4 of your Pearson Physics textbook.

Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation

Isaac Newton proposed that gravitation is a universal force that acts between all masses, no matter how big or small: $$F_g = {GMm \over r^2}$$

Newton derived this equation by applying Kepler’s Laws to circular planetary orbits.

This is an inverse square law; as the separation between masses increases, the gravitational attraction gets weaker in proportion to the square of the distance.

The universal gravitational constant was measured by Cavendish’s torsion balance experiment. The modern value is:$$G = 6.67\times 10^{-11} {\mathrm {N\cdot m^2\over kg^2}}$$

Cavendish Experiment

A torsion balance is a device that will twist when a force is applied, until the wire’s tendency to untwist balances the force. The twist angle (\(\theta\)) tells us how strong the force is.

Henry Cavendish used a large torsion balance to measure the force of gravity (\(F_g\)) between the masses (\(M, m\)) as he manipulated the separation (\(r\)) between them.

His data confirmed that gravitational force obeys the inverse square law that Newton predicted:$$F_g = {GMm \over r^2}$$

The results of Cavendish’s experiment gave a calculated value of \(G\) within about 1% of the modern value.

Gravitational Fields

Newton originally understood gravity as an example of action at a distance. Every mass in the universe somehow reaches out over large distances to exert a force on every other mass.

$${\vec {\mathbf a}} = {{\vec {\mathbf F}}_g \over m} = {GM \over r^2} = {\vec {\mathbf g}}$$

Orbital Motion

For an object moving in a circular orbit around a large mass like a planet or star, we can write: $$g = {GM\over r^2}$$ $$a = {v^2\over r} = {4\pi^2 r\over T^2}$$

Since gravity is the only force acting on an object, the variables \(g\) and \(a\) are equal. This means we can substitute the equation for \(g\) into an equation for \(a\).
