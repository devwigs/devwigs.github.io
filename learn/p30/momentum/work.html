Mechanical Forms of Energy

Types of energy that are related to the objects’ motion are called mechanical forms of energy. Mechanical energy includes:

Kinetic Energy\(E_k = {1 \over 2} mv^2\)
Gravitational Potential Energy\(E_g = mgh\)
Elastic Potential Energy\(E_{elas} = {1\over 2}k \left({\Delta L}\right)^2\)
Rotational EnergyDepends on object
  • Mass (\(m\)) is measured in kilograms.
  • Speed (\(v\)) is measured in m/s.
  • Height (\(h\)) and length (\(L\)) are measured in metres.
  • The elastic constant (\(k\)) is measured in N/m.
  • Gravitational field strength (\(g\)) is measured in m/s2.

Scientists use the term “mechanical energy” to mean the total of all mechanical forms of energy present in a system at a particular instant in time.

Some non-mechanical forms of energy include:

  • Thermal energy
  • Sound energy
  • Electrical energy
  • Chemical energy
  • Nuclear energy
  • Light and other electromagnetic energy

Energy Conservation

The Law of Energy Conservation is one of the most important laws in all of Science.

  • Energy cannot be created or destroyed.
  • The total energy must always remain constant.
  • Energy can be transformed from one form to another.
  • Energy can be transferred to different objects or locations.
  • All energy gains must be perfectly balanced by energy losses elsewhere.

When a system is not isolated, we must account for energy entering or leaving the system.

  • Energy that will be added to the system is included with the initial energy.
  • Energy that has been removed from the system is included with the final energy.

Energy Transformations

Changes in mechanical energy are caused by forces.

  • Gravitational force transforms gravitational potential energy into kinetic energy or vice versa.
  • An applied force transforms some non-mechanical energy in kinetic energy.
  • Friction and air resistance forces transform kinetic energy to “waste” (thermal and/or sound energy).

Work-Energy Theorem

The amount of kinetic energy gained due to a force acting on an object is called work.

  • A negative amount of work means that kinetic energy is lost.

According to the Work-Energy Theorem, we can calculate the work using the equation:$$W = \Delta E_k = {\vec{\mathbf{F}}} \cdot \Delta{\vec{\mathbf{d}}}$$

For 2D motion, the equation becomes:$$W = \Delta E_k = \left|{\vec{\mathbf{F}}}\right| \cdot \left|\Delta{\vec{\mathbf{d}}}\right| \cos(\Delta\theta)$$
