7.1 – Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation

Acceleration Due to Gravity

Earth (and other planets) orbit around the Sun in (nearly) circular orbits.

The Sun’s gravity causes the centripetal acceleration of the planets:

$$g=a_c={4\pi^2 r\over T^2}$$

According to Kepler’s 3rd Law, the orbital period is related to the orbital radius:

$$T^2=kr^3$$

We can eliminate \(T\) by substitution:

$$g={4\pi^2 r\over kr^3}$$

Simplifying:

$$g={4\pi^2\over kr^2}$$

Inverse Square Law (1)

$$g={4\pi^2\over kr^2}$$

Because of the \(r^2\) in the denominator, gravity is an inverse square law.

This means that an object that is twice as far from the Sun as Earth will experience a gravitational acceleration that is four times smaller.

An object three times as far away will experience a gravitational acceleration that is nine times smaller.

Inverse Square Law (2)

$$g={4\pi^2\over kr^2}$$

The Kepler constant, \(k\), is a property of the Sun.

Newton reasoned that if the Sun had a larger mass, its gravity would be proportionally stronger.

For example, if the Sun had double the mass, its gravity should be twice as strong.

Since \(k\) is related to \(M\), we should be able to write the equation in terms of the Sun’s mass rather than \(k\):

$$g={GM\over r^2}$$

The constant \(G\) is called the universal gravitational constant.

The value of \(G\) was first measured more than a century after Newton’s death: $$G = 6.67\times 10^{-11} {\mathrm {N\cdot m^2\over kg^2}}$$

Universal Gravitation

$$g={GM\over r^2}$$

The variable \(g\) represents the acceleration of the planet or other orbiting object due to the Sun’s gravity.

To calculate the gravitational force we need to multiply by the object’s mass, according to Newton’s 2nd Law:

$$F_g=mg$$

Combining the equations:

$$F_g={GMm\over r^2}$$

In this equation, \(M\) is the mass of the Sun, which is the source of the gravity.

The small \(m\) is the test mass; the mass of Earth, or any other object that is experiencing the Sun’s gravitational pull.
