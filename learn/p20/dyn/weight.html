3.3 – Mass & Weight

Gravitational Field

Galileo observed that all objects moving under gravity have the same acceleration when there are no other forces affecting the motion (e.g. air resistance or applied forces.)

  • On the surface of Earth, the value of the acceleration of gravity (also called the gravitational field strength) is, on average \(|{\vec{\mathbf g}}| = {\mathrm{9.81\ m/s^2}}\).
  • The actual value of \(|{\vec{\mathbf g}}|\) on Earth varies slightly depending on altitude and latitude.
  • On the Moon, the approximate value of \(|{\vec{\mathbf g}}|\) is \({\mathrm{1.63\ m/s^2}}\).

Mass & Weight

The mass (\(m\)) of an object describes the:

  • amount of matter that the object contains
  • how much inertia the object possesses (how difficult it is to change the object’s motion).

Mass does not depend on gravity: a 1 kg mass on the Moon contains just as much matter as the same 1 kg object on Earth.

An object’s weight (\({\vec{\mathbf F}}_g\)) is the force that gravity exerts on the object.

  • Weight depends on both the object’s mass and on the gravitational field strength.
  • Since the acceleration is \(\vec{\mathbf g}\) when gravity is the only force acting, according to Newton’s 2nd Law the relationship between mass and weight is:$${\vec{\mathbf F}}_g = m{\vec{\mathbf g}}$$
  • This means for example, that a 1.00 kg object weighs 9.81 N on Earth.
  • The same 1.00 kg object when placed on the Moon weighs only 1.63 N.
  • A 1.00 kg object in deep space, far from any sources of gravity, has no weight at all.

Apparent Weight

It is not possible to sense or feel the force of gravity in a uniform gravitational field.

Because uniform gravity acts equally on all parts of your body, it does not create any stresses in your tissues that your body is able to feel.

Ironically, the sensation of having weight is created by all of the forces acting on you except for gravity.

  • When you are standing, the normal force of the floor acting on your feet creates stress in your ankles, knees, etc. that gives your body the sensation of weight.
  • When you are accelerating, the forces acting on you are not balanced, and you may feel the sensation of increased or decreased apparent weight.
  • For example, you feel heavy when you begin to ascend in an elevator, and light when the elevator begins to slow down.
