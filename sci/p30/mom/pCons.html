Momentum Conservation

We saw in the previous lesson that impulse is a transfer of momentum.

  • Due to Newton’s 3rd Law, the change in one object’s momentum will always be balanced by an equal and opposite change to the other object.
  • The total momentum will always be conserved! $$\Sigma{\vec{\mathbf p}_f} = \Sigma{\vec{\mathbf p}_i}$$

Problem Solving

$$\Sigma{\vec{\mathbf p}_f} = \Sigma{\vec{\mathbf p}_i}$$

Momentum conservation is most useful for isolated systems and systems where external forces are balanced.

  • Frictionless collisions, rocket propulsion, and explosions are some of the most common uses for momentum conservation.
  • Forces that objects within the system exert on each other are internal forces. Internal forces do not change the total momentum of the system.
  • Forces that an object within the system experiences due to the environment (e.g. Earth) are external forces.
  • Unbalanced external forces will transfer momentum into or out of the system (e.g. to or from the Earth!)
  • Always draw a free body diagram when there is more than one force involved.

Example 1

A bumper car of mass 200 kg (including the rider) moves at +3.00 m/s when it collides head-on with a second bumper car of mass 150 kg and velocity −2.50 m/s. After the collision the first car has slowed to +0.500 m/s.

  • Calculate the final velocity of the second bumper car.

Use \({\vec {\mathbf p}} = m{\vec {\mathbf v}}\) to calculate all initial and final momenta for which both the mass and velocity are known... $${\vec{\mathbf p}_{Ai}} = \mathrm{200\ kg\ (+3.00\ m/s) = +600\ N\cdot s}$$

$${\vec{\mathbf p}_{Bi}} = \mathrm{150\ kg\ (-2.50\ m/s) = -375\ N\cdot s}$$

$${\vec{\mathbf p}_{Af}} = \mathrm{200\ kg\ (+0.500\ m/s) = +100\ N\cdot s}$$

Initial Final
\(\vec{\mathbf p}_A\) \(\mathrm{+600\ N\cdot s}\) \(\mathrm{+100\ N\cdot s}\)
\(\vec{\mathbf p}_B\) \(\mathrm{-375\ N\cdot s}\) \(\mathrm{+125\ N\cdot s}\)
\(\Sigma\vec{\mathbf p}\) \(\mathrm{+225\ N\cdot s}\)

Calculate the total momentum of the bumper cars using the initial momenta... $$\Sigma\vec{\mathbf p} = \mathrm{(+600 + (-375))\ N\cdot s = +225\ N\cdot s}$$

Use the fact that total momentum is conserved to find the remaining final momentum... $$\Sigma\vec{\mathbf p} = {\vec{\mathbf p}_{Bf}} + {\vec{\mathbf p}_{Af}}$$

$${\vec{\mathbf p}_{Bf}} = \Sigma\vec{\mathbf p} - {\vec{\mathbf p}_{Af}} = \mathrm{(+225 - 100)\ N\cdot s = +125\ N\cdot s}$$

Use the momentum to find the final velocity of “B”: $${\vec {\mathbf v}_{Bf}} = {{\vec {\mathbf p}_{Bf}}\over m_B} = {\mathrm{+125\ N\cdot s\over 150\ kg}} = {\mathrm{0.833\ m/s}}$$

Example 2

A train with a mass of 20.0 t is moving along the track at 4.00 m/s. The train collides with a railcar that has a mass of 5.00 t and is at rest. The cars couple together as a result of the collision.

  • Calculate the final velocity of the coupled train.

Calculate the initial and total momentum of the trains. $${\vec{\mathbf p}_{Ai}} = \mathrm{(20.0\times 10^3\ kg)\ (+4.00\ m/s) = +80.0\ kN\cdot s}$$

Initial Final
\(\vec{\mathbf p}_A\) \(\mathrm{+80.0\ kN\cdot s}\) \(\mathrm{+64.0\ kN\cdot s}\)
\(\vec{\mathbf p}_B\) \(\mathrm{0.00\ kN\cdot s}\) \(\mathrm{+16.0\ kN\cdot s}\)
\(\Sigma\vec{\mathbf p}\) \(\mathrm{+80.0\ kN\cdot s}\)

Create an equation that represents the fact that total momentum is conserved: $$\Sigma{\vec {\mathbf p}} = m_A {\vec {\mathbf v}_f} + m_B {\vec {\mathbf v}_f}$$

Factor out the common factor; \(\vec{\mathbf v}_f\) is the same for both masses when they are stuck together:$$\Sigma{\vec {\mathbf p}} = (m_A + m_B) {\vec {\mathbf v}_f}$$

Solve for the final velocity:$${\vec {\mathbf v}_f} = {\Sigma{\vec {\mathbf p}} \over m_A + m_B} = {\mathrm{{+80000\ N\cdot s \over 25000\ kg} = +3.20\ m/s}}$$

  • Calculate the change in mechanical energy due to the collision. Where did the missing energy go?

Calculate the initial energy of the system... $$\Sigma E_i = \frac{1}{2}m_A v_{Ai}^2 = \frac{1}{2}\mathrm{(20.0\times 10^3\ kg) (4.00\ m/s)^2 = 160\ kJ}$$

Calculate the final energy of the system... $$E_{Af} = \frac{1}{2}m_A v_f^2 = \frac{1}{2}\mathrm{(20.0\times 10^3\ kg) (3.20\ m/s)^2 = 102.4\ kJ}$$

$$E_{Bf} = \frac{1}{2}m_B v_f^2 = \frac{1}{2}\mathrm{(5.00\times 10^3\ kg) (3.20\ m/s)^2 = 25.6\ kJ}$$

$$\Sigma E_f = \mathrm{(102.4 + 25.6)\ kJ = 128\ kJ}$$

Calculate the change in energy... $$\Delta E = \Sigma E_f - \Sigma E_i = \mathrm{(128 - 160)\ kJ = -32.0\ kJ}$$

This 32.0 kilojoules represents the sound and thermal energy generated by the collision.
