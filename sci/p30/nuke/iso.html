Mass Spectrometer

A mass spectrometer is a device used for measuring the mass of ions based on the principles of Thomson’s experiment.

  • A potential difference is used to accelerate the ions into a beam
  • If the ions all start from rest at the positive plate, then we can use the work-energy theorem to calculate the beam speed: $$qV_{acc} = W = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$
  • Some mass spectrometer designs use a velocity selector.
  • As in Thomson’s experiment, electric and magnetic fields create forces in opposite directions.
  • The forces will balance when the particles have the desired speed: $$qvB = F_m = F_e = q|\vec{\mathbf E}|$$ $$v = \frac{|\vec{\mathbf E}|}{B}$$
  • Particles moving faster than this will experience a stronger magnetic force and be deflected; slower particles will be deflected in the opposite direction because the magnetic force will be weaker.
  • Undeflected particles pass into a detector where there is uniform magnetic field.
  • The mass of the particles can be determined based on the radius of the uniform circular motion: $$F_m = ma$$ $$qvB = m\frac{v^2}{r}$$ $$m = \frac{qBr}{v}$$
  • A fluorescent screen or photographic film can be used to detect where the particle hit.
  • Modern devices use electronic detectors (like a digital camera) to measure the radius of the particles’ trajectory.

Isotopes

The nucleus contains the atom’s positive charge, in the form of particles called protons.

  • In a neutral atom, the number of protons and electrons are equal.
  • Why don’t the protons repel each other?
  • If helium (for example) has two protons to balance its two electrons, why is its mass four times that of hydrogen?
  • Rutherford’s nucleus also contains a second type of particle called a neutron.
  • Neutrons have approximately the same mass as a proton, but no electric charge.
  • Neutrons (somehow!) prevent the protons from repelling each other due to their positive charge. The force that overpowers the electric repulsion is called the strong nuclear force.
  • Neutrons were detected experimentally by James Chadwick in 1932.
  • Because neutrons have no charge, the number of neutrons does not affect the number of electrons, or the chemical behaviour of an atom.
  • Chemically identical atoms with the same number of protons and different numbers of neutrons are called isotopes.

Atomic Number & Mass Number

The number of protons determines the atomic number of a nuclide.

  • Atomic number determines which element a nuclide belongs to, and thus its chemical behaviour.
  • The total number of nucleons (i.e protons and neutrons) is called the mass number or atomic mass.
  • Molar mass (which you use in Chemistry) is the average of the mass numbers of all natural isotopes of an element.
  • Most carbon atoms in nature are carbon-12, but a small percentage are carbon-13, giving carbon a molar mass of 12.01 g/mol.
  • Do not use molar mass in nuclear physics, as we are interested in the separate isotopes, not average chemical properties.
  • In isotope notation, the chemical symbol for an element is written with the atomic number to the lower left and the atomic mass to the upper left.
  • For example, the symbol for carbon-13 is $$\Large{_{\ 6}^{13}}{\mathrm C}$$
  • Carbon-13 has 6 protons (atomic number) and 7 neutrons (\(13-6\)).
