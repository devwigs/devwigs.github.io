Ray Model

Because light has a very small wavelength, in most situations it does not diffract significantly.

  • The ray model of light assumes that light can be treated as travelling in straight lines, except where it reflects or refracts from a boundary.
  • When light rays change direction due to reflection (mirror) or refraction (lens), light may appear to originate from a place other than its actual origin.

Image Formation

The point where the reflected/refracted light appears to originate is the image of the object where the light actually originates.


[Interactive Diagram]

  • This ray diagram shows refraction of light from a submerged object at the surface of the pond.
  • A virtual image is produced at a shallower depth than the actual depth of the object.
  • If reflected/refracted light rays actually cross to form an image, the image is said to be real.
  • Real images can be projected onto a screen (e.g. as in a movie projector or a camera.)
  • When the light rays do not actually cross at the image’s location, the image is called virtual.
  • Virtual images cannot be projected onto a screen.
  • Reflected/refracted rays that are extended backwards to locate a virtual image are called virtual rays.
  • When all the reflected/refracted rays from a point object converge to form an image at a single point, the image will be sharp.
  • A fuzzy image is produced when the rays from a single point converge over a region larger than a single point.
  • A ray diagram is an illustration of the incident and reflected/refracted rays that we use to locate the image.
  • To draw a ray diagram, you will need a protractor to ensure that the angle of reflection equals the angle of incidence. [Interactive Diagram]
  • For refraction, you will need to use the Snell’s Law formula to find the angle of refraction before you can draw it.
