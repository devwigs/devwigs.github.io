2.5 – Electric Potential Difference

Electric Potential Difference

When a charged object is placed in an electric field, the electric force may do work on the charge by causing it to speed up or slow down.

Charged objects within an electric field have electric potential energy because the field has the “ability to do work” on the charge.

$$-\Delta E_p = W = {\vec{\mathbf F}}_e\cdot\Delta{\vec{\mathbf d}}$$

  • The work done by the field on a test charge is calculated using the work-energy theorem:$$\Delta E_k = -\Delta E_p = W = {\vec{\mathbf F}}_e\cdot\Delta{\vec{\mathbf d}}$$
  • A positive work indicates an increase in kinetic energy and a decrease in potential energy.
  • If the field is uniform, we can substitute \({\vec{\mathbf F}}_e = q{\vec{\mathbf E}}\):$$-\Delta E_p = W = q{\vec{\mathbf E}}\cdot\Delta{\vec{\mathbf d}}$$

$$-\Delta E_p = W = q{\vec{\mathbf E}}\cdot\Delta{\vec{\mathbf d}}$$

Physicists define the electric potential difference, \(\Delta V\), as the work done per unit of test charge:

$$-\Delta V = {W\over q} = {\vec{\mathbf E}}\cdot\Delta{\vec{\mathbf d}}$$

  • Do not confuse electric field (\(\vec{\mathbf E}\)) with energy (\(E\)).
  • This equation is just the work-energy theorem expressed ‘per Coulomb’ rather than as a total! I.e. potential difference is the work per Coulomb while the electric field is the force per Coulomb.
  • The unit of potential difference is J/C, which is called a volt. Potential difference is sometimes just called the ‘Voltage’.
  • When the field is not constant, potential diffference is the area under a graph of \(\vec{\mathbf E}\) vs \(\vec{\mathbf d}\).

  • Note that force also relates to the test charge’s acceleration while the work relates to its kinetic energy and speed: $${\vec{\mathbf F}}_{net} = m{\vec{\mathbf a}}$$ $$W = \Delta E_k = {1\over 2}mv_f^2 - {1\over 2}mv_i^2$$

Electron-volt

The electron-volt is defined as the amount of work done on an electron by a potential difference of 1 volt: $${\mathrm{eV = 1.60\times 10^{-19} C\cdot 1 V = 1.60\times 10^{-19} J}}$$

  • The electron-volt is often used as a unit of energy when dealing with atomic scale problems.
