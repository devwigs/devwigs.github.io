Net electric charge is the difference between the number of protons and electrons in an object.

  • Neutral objects can become charged by friction, conduction, or induction.
  • Opposite charges attract; like charges repel.
  • Neutral objects that can be polarized will attract to both positive and negative charges.

Electric charge is a conserved quantity.

  • The standard SI unit of electric charge is the Coulomb (C).
  • The charge of a single proton or electron is called the elementary charge (\(+e\) for protons and \(–e\) for electrons): $$1 e = 1.60\times 10^{–19}\ \mathrm{C}$$
  • Millikan determined the elementary charge by balancing the electric and gravitational forces on an oil drop.

The force between two uniform, sphere-shaped charges, is given by Coulomb’s Law: $$|{\vec{\mathbf F}}_e| = \frac{kq_1q_2}{r^2}$$

  • The value of Coulomb’s constant is determined experimentally to be $$k = 8.99\times 10^9\ \mathrm{\frac{N\cdot m^2}{C^2}}$$
  • For multiple charges, the net electric force is the vector total of the electric force due to each individual field charge.

The electric field is defined as the ratio of the electric force to the test charge: $$\vec{\mathbf E} = \frac{\vec{\mathbf F}_e}{q}$$

  • The electric field of a sphere-shaped source (\(Q\)) is: $$|\vec{\mathbf E}| = \frac{kQ}{r^2$}$
  • The direction of \(\vec{\mathbf E}\) indicates the direction of \(\vec{\mathbf F}_e\) acting on positive test charges. For negative test charges, \(\vec{\mathbf F}_e\) is opposite to \(\vec{\mathbf E}\).

On an electric field diagram arrows show the direction of the force on positive test charges.

  • Field lines point away from positive field charges and towards negative field charges.
  • The strength of \(\vec{\mathbf E}\) is indicated by how close together the field lines are.
  • “Crossing points” are places where the net electric field is zero.

A (nearly) uniform electric field is produced by two parallel, oppositely charged conducting plates.

  • Coulomb’s formula does not apply because the charged objects are not spherical!

Test charges within the electric field have electric potential energy.

  • The electric field does work (transforms energy between electric potential and kinetic) when the test charges move.
  • Electric potential difference is defined as the ratio of the work done by the electric field, compared to the test charge: $$\Delta V = \frac{\Delta E}{q} = \frac{{\vec{\mathbf F}}_e\cdot\Delta\vec{\mathbf d}}{q} = \vec{\mathbf E}\cdot\Delta\vec{\mathbf d}$$
  • The unit of potential difference is the volt: \(\mathrm{1\ V = 1\ J/C}\).
  • Potential difference is also called voltage.
  • For a test charge moving in a uniform field, the motion (e.g. velocity and acceleration) can be determined using uniform accelerated motion, Newton’s Laws, and/or energy conservation.
