Electric Field Diagrams

An electric field diagram illustrates the electric field produced by one or more source charges “everywhere”.

  • The direction of the field line at any point indicates the direction of the electric force acting on a small positive test charge placed at that point.
  • Negative test charges would experience a force in the opposite direction.
  • Electric field diagrams are always drawn assuming a positive test charge.
  • The density of field lines indicates the strength of the field; the field lines get closer together where the field is stronger.
  • Field lines always diverge (point away) from positive sources (which repel the positive test charges) and converge at negative sources (which attract the positive test charges.)

When sketching the electric field without the help of a computer, it is best to start by drawing any lines of symmetry.

  • A crossing point (a point where half of the field lines converge and half diverge) is an equilibrium: a point where the net electric force on a test charge will be zero.

Field as Mediator

The idea of a field (electric, gravitational, or magnetic) is an alternative to action at a distance.

  • With action at a distance, two charges (or masses) need to “reach out” over possibly large distances to exert force on each other.
  • With field theory, each charge affects the “field” around it, and each charge experiences a force due to the field of all other sources. The electric field mediates the electric force.
  • The object experiencing a field is called a test charge.
  • The objects producing the field are called sources.
  • One physical difference between action at a distance and field theory is that changes to the field propoagate at finite speed.

Mathematics of Electric Field

The Coulomb force between a source \(Q\) and test charge \(q\) is:$$|{\vec{\mathbf F}_e}| = \left|{kQq\over r^2}\right|$$

We can rearrange this as:

$$\left|{\vec{\mathbf F}_e\over q}\right| = \left|{kQ\over r^2}\right|$$

The left side of this equation is taken as the mathematical definition of electric field:

$${\vec{\mathbf E}} = {{\vec{\mathbf F}_e}\over q}$$

This is analogous to our defintion of the gravitational field:$${\vec{\mathbf g}} = {{\vec{\mathbf F}}_g\over m}$$

  • The reason we define the field this way is that it gives the same value regardless of the test charge or mass.

The right side tells us the contribution that the source \(Q\) makes to the electric field:

$$|{\vec{\mathbf E}}| = {k|Q|\over r^2}$$

  • The SI unit of electric field is newtons per coluomb (N/C).
  • The electric force has the same direction as \({\vec{\mathbf E}}\) when the test charge (\(q\)) is positive and the opposite direction when the test charge is negative.
  • The direction of \({\vec{\mathbf E}}\) is directly toward a negative source or away from a positive source.
  • When more than one source is present, \({\vec{\mathbf E}}\) is the vector sum of each source’s contribution.

Example

Sources of +5.00 nC and −3.00 nC are separated by 12.0 cm. Calculate the electric field at: (a) the midpoint, and (b) a point 6.00 cm from the positive charge in a perpendicular direction.

Applications of Electric Fields

A parallel plate capacitor is used to generate a (nearly) uniform electric field.

  • The field is non-uniform near the edeges (“edge effects”).

An electrical discharge (e.g. lightning) occurs when the field becomes strong enough to ionize air molecules.

A conductor placed in an electric field will become polarized in such a way that the net electric field inside the conductor will be zero.

  • This effect is called shielding.
