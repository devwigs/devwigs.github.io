Magnetism is a force that acts between moving charges (i.e. currents).

  • Charges in motion produce both electric (\(\vec{\mathbf E}\)) and magnetic (\(\vec{\mathbf B}\)) fields.
  • Charges at rest produce \(\vec{\mathbf E}\) only.
  • Ampere’s Law states that the magnetic field strength is directly proportional to the current that produces it.
  • Gauss’s Law states that magnetic fields form closed loops with no convergence or divergence points.
  • The direction of the magnetic field tells us the direction that the north end of a compass will point: it is not the direction of the magnetic force.

First Hand Rule: The current in a long, straight wire produces a magnetic field pattern of circular loops centred on the wire.

  • Point your left thumb in the direction of electron flow; your fingers will curl in the direction of the magnetic field loops.
  • Use your right hand instead when the source is made of positive charges.

Second Hand Rule: The current in a coil of wire produces a dipolar magnetic field, similar to that of a bar magnet.

  • Curl your left fingers in the direction of electron flow; your thumb will point to the field’s “north” pole (defined as the direction that \(\vec{\mathbf B}\) points along the loop’s axis.)
  • Use your right hand when the source is made of positive charges.

The Third Hand Rule is used to find the direction of the magnetic force acting on a moving test charge or a current that is located within a magnetic field.

  • Point your left thumb in the direction of the electron flow.
  • Point your fingers in the direction of \(\vec{\mathbf B}\).
  • Your palm indicates the direction of \(\vec{\mathbf F}_m\), the magnetic force.
  • Use your right hand when the test charge/current is made of positive charges.

The magnitude of the magnetic force acting on a moving test charge is: $$|\vec{\mathbf F}_m| = qvB_\perp$$

  • This is the equation for the magnetic force acting on a charge \(q\) moving with speed \(v\).
  • The direction of \(\vec{\mathbf F}_m\) is given by the third hand rule.
  • The subscript ⊥ is an abbreviation for \(\sin\theta\), where \(\theta\) is the angle between the directions of \(\vec{\mathbf B}\) and \(\vec{\mathbf v}\).
  • The total magnetic force acting on a current-carrying wire is: $$|\vec{\mathbf F}_m| = ILB_\perp$$
  • \(I\) is the current and \(L\) is the length of wire within the magnetic field.

The motor principle is the observation that a dipole magnet (e.g. a coil or bar magnet) will try to orient itself so its north pole aligns with the external magnetic field.

  • A DC motor converts electrical energy into mechanical energy; the coil will rotate until it aligns with the external field.
  • A split-ring commutator and brushes are used to reverse the direction of the current whenever the coil reaches alignment; this keeps the motor spinning indefintely!
  • A galvanometer is another application of the motor principle; it measures the strength of the magnetic force trying to rotate the coil, which in turn tells us how much current is flowing through the coil.

A generator is the reverse of a motor; it uses magnetic forces to convert mechanical energy into electrical energy.

  • The phenomenon of using magnetic fields to “induce” an electromotive force (voltage) is called electromagnetic induction.
  • Faraday’s Law states that the EMF produced in a coil is equal to the rate at which the “magnetic flux” changes. This means that an EMF will be produced whenever one of the following variables for the coil changes:
    • the area of the coil
    • the average magnetic field experienced by the coil
    • the orientation of the coil relative to the field
  • According to Lenz’s Law, when a current is induced it will experience a magnetic force in the opposite direction to the direction of motion.
  • Lenz’s Law is simply the law of energy conservation applied to EM induction.
  • Most generators consist of a coil of wire that is rotated through a magnetic field.
  • The direction of the induced current reverses every half turn of the coil: this is called alternating current.
  • A split-ring commutator and brushes can be used keep the direction of the current constant; however this is often unnecessay since many electrical devices operate on AC.
