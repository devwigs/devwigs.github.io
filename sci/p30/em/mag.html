Physics 30

Electric Current

A flow of electric charge is called an electric current.

  • Current (\(I\)) is defined mathematically as:$$I = {\Delta Q\over\Delta t}$$
  • The SI unit of current is the ampere (A = C/s).

Electromagnetism

Hans Christian Oersted discovered that electric currents deflected the needle of a magnetic compass. This phenomenon is called electromagnetism.

  • Magnetism refers to a force that acts between electric charges only when the charges are in motion.
  • This “magnetic force” is in addition to the electric force that acts between charges even when they are not moving.
  • Some materials have magnetic properties:
    • Materials that can be permanently magnetized are called ferromagnetic. Iron and nickel are two of the most common ferromagnetic materials.
    • By contrast, paramagnetic materials become magnetized when another magnet is nearby, but return to being unmagnetized when the nearby magnet is removed.

Magnetic Fields

As with gravitational and electric forces, physicists use the concept of a field to understand how magnetic forces work.

  • Moving charges (i.e. electric currents) produce a magnetic field. This magnetic field then exerts a magnetic force on other nearby moving charges.
  • The symbol for magnetic field is \({\vec{\mathbf B}}\).
  • The SI unit is the tesla: T = N / (A·m).
  • A diagram of a magnetic field illustrates the direction that a compass needle will point when placed at each position within the field.

  • As with electric and gravitational field diagrams, the strength of the field corresponds to the density of field lines.
  • Like gravitational and electric fields, magnetic field is a vector.
  • A unique property of magnetic fields is that there are no points of convergence or divergence: magnetic field lines always form closed loops with no beginning or end!
  • The crosses in the diagram indicate a wire with a flow of electons away (into the page).
  • The dots in the diagram indicate a wire with a flow of electons toward (out of the page).

Ampere’s Law states that the strength of a magnetic field is directly proportional to the current that produces it.

  • Doubling the current flowing through a wire will double the magnetic field strength.

Measuring Magnetic Fields

While a compass will show the direction of the magnetic field, it is not an adequate device for making scientific measurements.

  • A compass does not allow for measurement of the magnitude of the magnetic field.
  • Since a compass needle often floats in water, it can only detect the horizontal component of the field.
  • Strong magnetic fields may change the magnetization of a compass needle, making it unreliable even for measuring directions.
  • Magnetic fields are measured using the “Hall Effect”.

  • When there is a magnetic field present, it causes the moving electrons in a current to be pushed toward one side of the wire.
  • This results in one side of the wire becoming positive and one side becoming negative.
  • The potential difference between the two sides of the wire tells us how strong the magnetic field is.
