2.1 – Electrostatics

Electric Charge

Matter is made up of protons, neutrons, and electrons.

  • Protons have a positive charge.
  • Electrons have a negative charge.
  • Neutrons have no charge.
  • Objects that contain equal numbers of protons and electrons are neutral.
  • Objects that have an excess of electrons are negatively charged.
  • Objects that have a defecit of electrons (i.e. more protons) are positively charged.

For everyday charged objects, the difference between the protons and electrons is relatively small.

  • An excess of a single electron for every billion protons can produce a very large net charge!

The SI unit of electric charge is the coulomb (C).

  • A coulomb is defined as the amount of charge carried by a 1-ampere current in 1 second.
  • The charge of a single proton or electron is called the elementary charge unit (e).
  • The conversion factor is:$$1\ e = 1.60\times 10^{-19}\ {\mathrm C}$$
  • A coulomb is a very large amount of charge; we often use microcoulombs (μC) or even nanocoulombs (nC).

Objects with opposite sign charges attract; objects with the same sign charge repel.

Like energy and momentum, charge is a conserved quantity.

Charging Methods

There are three common methods for creating charged objects:

  • Friction: Two different materials are rubbed against each other. The material with the greater affinity for electrons becomes negative and the other becomes positive.
  • Conduction: A neutral object is placed in contact with a charged object so that the net charge is shared. Both objects end up with the same sign charge.
  • Induction: A charged object is used to attract the opposite sign charge onto a neutral object from a third object (which is often the Earth.)

Conduction

Objects that permit the movement of charge easily are known as conductors.

Objects that prevent the movement of charge are known as insulators.

Objects that allow the movement of charge with significant loss of energy are known as resistors.

Polarization

When a neutral object is placed near a charged object, electrons may move within the object to form two poles.

  • The negative pole will be the region where electrons are attracted to.
  • The positive pole will be the region where electrons are repeled from.
  • The pole closest to the charged object will be the one that has the opposite sign.
  • A polarized object will attract to charges of either sign.
  • Some substances, including water, methanol, and ethanol, have a permanent polarization due to their molecular structure.

Electroscope

A metal-leaf electroscope is a device for detecting charge.

  • A charged object brough close to the top of the electroscope will polarize it.
  • The ball will have the opposite sign as the charge, and the leaves will have the same sign.
  • The leaves will repel each other due to having the same sign.

If the electroscope is given a net charge, the ball and leaves will all have the same sign as the charge spreads out as much as it can due to repulsion.

  • If the same sign charge is brought near the top, the leaves will become more charged and spread farther apart.
  • If the opposite sign charge is brought near the top, the leaves will become less charged and will become less separated.
  • The leaves will never have opposite signs, and therefore will never attract past vertical.
