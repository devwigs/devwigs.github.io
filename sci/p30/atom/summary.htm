Dalton’s Billiard Ball Model proposes that atoms are featureless spheres.

  • Different elements have atoms of different sizes.
  • This model explains the Law of Definite Proportions and is the basis of Kinertic-Molecular Theory (which explains phenomena such as temperature and the ideal gas law).

Thomson’s CRT Experiment studied how uniform electric and magnetic fields affected a beam of particles in a cathode ray tube.

  • The particles are accelerated into a beam by an accelerating potential: $$q\Delta V_{acc} = W = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$
  • A deflecting potential creates an electric field that deflects the particles perpendicular to their direction of motion.
  • A magnetic field creates a force in the opposite direction to the electric force.
  • When the fields are adjusted so the forces balance: $$q|\vec{\mathbf E}| = F_e = F_m = qvB$$
  • This allows the speed of the particles to be calculated.
  • Turning off the deflecting electric field results in uniform circular motion through the magnetic field: $$F_m = ma$$ $$qvB = m\frac{v^2}{r}$$ $$\frac{q}{m} = \frac{v}{Br}$$

When Thomson’s CRT experiment is performed on both negative and positive charges:

  • The negative particles (electrons) have much smaller masses than the positive charges.
  • Electrons are identical in all elements.
  • The positive parts are not identical in different elements.

Thomson’s Raisin Bun Model proposes that atoms are like a positively-charged bun with electrons embedded in it like raisins.

  • Atoms of different elements have different sizes as in Dalton’s model.
  • Larger elements have more electrons.

Rutherford, Geiger, and Marsden scattered alpha particles (helium-4 nuclei produced by radioactive decay) from a thin foil of gold or aluminum.

  • Most alpha particles passed through undeflected, as expected.
  • A small fraction of the particles scattered to large angles, which could not be explained by the Raisin Bun model.

Rutherford proposed the Planetary (Nuclear) Model to replace the Raisin Bun.

  • A small but massive nucleus contains the atom’s positive charge and almost all of its mass.
  • Electrons orbit around the nucleus like planets around the Sun.
  • Most of the alpha particles were undeflected because they encountered only empty space.
  • Large deflections occur for the small fraction of alpha particles that collide head-on (or close to head-on) with the nucleus.
  • Analysis of the results shows that the diameter of the nucleus is \(10^4\) or \(10^5\) times smaller than the diameter of the atom.
  • Rutherford’s model does not explain why the orbiting electrons do not continuously emit EMR.

Niels Bohr modified the Planetary Model by adding the idea of energy levels.

  • Assuming that the angular momentum of the orbiting electrons was quantized, Bohr showed that electrons could only orbit in certain orbits with specific energies.
  • Electrons would only emit (or absorb) photons when they jumped from one energy level to another
  • Atoms can only emit certain (discrete) wavelengths that have photon energies corresponding to the difference between electron energy levels.
  • The calculated energy levels correctly predict the emission spectrum for hydrogen.
  • Atoms with more than one electron are more complicated due to the repulsion between electrons.

De Broglie hypothesized that wave-particle duality applies to electrons and other particles, which have a de Broglie wavelength: $$\lambda = \frac{h}{p}$$

  • If we treat the orbiting electron in the atom as a wave, a stationary state (that would not emit EMR) can only occur when the circumference of the orbit is an exact multiple of the de Broglie wavelength: $$2\pi r = n\left(\frac{h}{p}\right)$$
  • This explains why the angular momentum is quantized! $$rp = n\left(\frac{h}{2\pi}\right)$$

Direct evidence that electrons can behave as waves comes from interference patterns that occur with:

  • Diffraction of electrons from a crystal, as observed by Davisson and Germer;
  • Double-slit experiments, similar to Young’s experiments with light.
