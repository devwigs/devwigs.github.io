Science 10

Energy

Energy is “the ability to do work” or “the ability to create motion”.

Energy comes in many forms:

  • Chemical Energy is associated with the arrangement of atoms in a substance.
  • Nuclear Energy is associated with the arrangement of protons and neutrons within a nucleus.
  • Thermal Energy and Sound Waves are forms of energy associated with the movement of molecules within a substance.
  • Light (and other forms of “electromagnetic radiation” like x-rays and radio waves) is a form of energy.

All of the above are non-mechanical forms of energy because they are unrelated to an object’s motion.

Mechanical Energy

Mechanical forms of energy are types of energy that are related to an object’s motion.

There are two mechanical forms of energy that we will study in Science 10:

  • Kinetic energy
  • Gravitational potential energy

Other types of mechanical energy that we will study in Physics 20 include:

  • Rotational energy
  • Elastic potential energy

All forms of energy are scalar quantities.

The SI unit for energy is the joule (J).

Kinetic Energy

When an object is in motion, it has Kinetic Energy.

  • We can calculate kinetic energy using the equation:$$E_k={1\over 2}mv^2$$
  • \(m\) is the object’s mass in kilograms. A larger mass has more energy than a smaller mass when moving at the same speed.
  • \(v\) is the object’s speed in m/s. Objects have more energy when they are moving at faster speeds.

Gravitational Potential Energy

When an object is elevated above the ground, it has Gravitational Potential Energy.

  • We can calculate gravitational potential energy using the equation:$$E_g=mgh$$
  • \(m\) is the object’s mass in kilograms.
  • \(g\) is the strength of gravity, which is \(\mathrm{9.81 m/s^2}\) on Earth, near the ground.
  • \(h\) is the object’s height in metres above the origin.
  • When an object rises above the ground, gravity transforms its kinetic energy into gravitational potential energy.
  • When an object falls, gravity transforms its gravitational potential energy into kinetic energy.

Example 1

A player kicks a 600-gram soccer ball. When the ball is 2.00 metres above the ground, its speed is 3.00 m/s.

  • What is the ball’s kinetic energy?

$$\begin{align}E_k &= {1\over 2}mv^2 \\&= {1\over 2}\mathrm{0.600\ kg\cdot(3.00\ m/s)^2}\\&= \mathrm{2.70\ J}\end{align}$$

  • What is the ball’s gravitational potential energy?

$$\begin{align}E_g &= mgh \\&= \mathrm{0.600\ kg \cdot 9.81\ m/s^2 \cdot 2.00\ m}\\&= \mathrm{11.8\ J}\end{align}$$

  • How much mechanical energy does the ball have?

$$E = E_k + E_g = \mathrm{2.70\ J + 11.8\ J = 14.5\ J}$$

Example 2

Calculate the height of a 750-gram football when it has 20.0 joules of gravitational potential energy.

We need to use algebra to isolate the unknown (\(h\)) before we can complete the calculation:

$$E_g = mgh$$

$$\frac{E_g}{\color{red}{mg}} = \frac{mgh}{\color{red}{mg}}$$

$$\require{cancel}\frac{E_g}{\color{red}{mg}} = \frac{\cancel{mg}h}{\cancel{\color{red}{mg}}}$$

$$\frac{E_g}{mg} = h$$

$$h = \mathrm{\frac{20.0\ J}{0.750\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2} = 2.72\ m}$$

Example 3

Calculate the speed of the football when it has 35.0 joules of kinetic energy.

We need to use algebra to isolate the unknown (\(v\)) before we can complete the calculation:

$$E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$

$$\frac{\color{red}2 E_k}{\color{red}m} = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \cdot \color{red}{\frac{2}{m}}$$

$$\frac{\color{red}2 E_k}{\color{red}m} = \frac{1}{\cancel 2}\cancel mv^2 \cdot \color{red}{\frac{\cancel 2}{\cancel m}}$$

$$\frac{2E_k}{m} = v^2$$

To cancel the “squared”, we need to square root both sides:

$$\sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m}} = \sqrt{v^2} = v$$

$$v = \mathrm{\sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 35.0\ J}{0.700\ kg}} = 14.1\ m/s}$$
