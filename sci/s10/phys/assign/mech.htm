Practice

  1. Calculate the kinetic energy of a 52.8 kg runner moving with a speed of 5.60 m/s.
    2. $$\begin{align} E_k &= \frac{1}{2}mv^2 \\ &= \mathrm{\frac{1}{2} (52.8\ kg)(5.60\ m/s)^2} \\ &= \mathrm{828\ J} \end{align}$$
  2. A 0.300-kilogram apple hangs from a tree branch 2.75 m above the ground. Calculate the gravitational potential energy of the apple.
    3. $$E_g = mgh = \mathrm{(0.300\ kg) (9.81\ m/s^2) (2.75\ m) = 8.09\ J}$$
  3. A 624-gram soccer ball is kicked down the field. At the highest point of the ball’s motion, it has a height of 3.76 m and a speed of 7.21 m/s. Calculate the mechanical energy (i.e. the total of the kinetic and gravitational potential energies) of the ball. Remember to convert grams to kilograms before performing any calculations.
    4. $$E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \mathrm{\frac{1}{2} (0.624\ kg) (7.21\ m/s)^2 = 16.2\ J}$$ $$E_g = mgh = \mathrm{(0.624\ kg) (9.81\ m/s^2) (3.76\ m) = 23.0\ J}$$ $$\mathrm{Mechanical\ Energy} = E_k + E_g = \mathrm{16.2\ J + 23.0\ J = 39.2\ J}$$
  4. A 0.825 kg hammer is left on the roof of a house under construction. The hammer has 50.0 joules of gravitational potential energy. How high is the hammer above the ground?
    5. $$E_g = mgh$$ $$h = \frac{E_g}{mg} = \mathrm{\frac{50.0\ J}{0.825\ kg · 9.81\ m/s^2} = 6.18\ m}$$
    1. A 1900 kg car moves with a speed of 80.0 km/h. Convert this speed to standard SI units.
      2. $$\mathrm{80.0\ km/h = 80.0 \frac{10^3\ m}{3600\ s} = 22.2\ m/s}$$
    2. How much kinetic energy does the car have?
      3. $$E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \mathrm{\frac{1}{2} (1900\ kg) (22.2\ m/s)^2 = 4.69\times 10^5\ J}$$
    3. The car accelerates to 100 km/h. How much kinetic energy does the car have now?
      4. $$\mathrm{100\ km/h = 100 \frac{10^3\ m}{3600\ s} = 27.8\ m/s}$$ $$E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \mathrm{\frac{1}{2} (1900\ kg) (27.8\ m/s)^2 = 7.33\times 10^5\ J}$$
    4. Calculate the change in kinetic energy (\(\Delta E_k\)) that the car experienced while accelerating from 80.0 to 100 km/h?
      5. $$\Delta E_k = E_{kf} - E_{ki} = \mathrm{7.33\times 10^5\ J - 4.69\times 10^5\ J = 2.64\times 10^5\ J}$$
    5. Which non-mechanical form of energy was the source of this additional kinetic energy?
      6. The fuel (gasoline, diesel, propane) is chemical energy
  6. A 0.587 kg baseball rolls along the ground with 18.0 joules of kinetic energy. Calculate the speed of the baseball.
    7. $$E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$ $$\frac{2E_k}{m} = v^2$$ $$v = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m}} = \mathrm{\sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 18.0\ J}{0.587\ kg}} = 7.83\ m/s}$$
    1. A 68.0 kg cyclist is riding at 6.20 m/s along a path at the top of a 12.0-metre high hill. Calculate the cyclist’s mechanical energy.
      2. $$E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \mathrm{\frac{1}{2} (68.0\ kg) (6.20\ m/s)^2 = 1.31\times 10^3\ J}$$ $$E_g = mgh = \mathrm{(68.0\ kg) (9.81\ m/s^2) (12.0\ m) = 8.00\times 10^3\ J}$$ $$\begin{align} \mathrm{Mechanical\ Energy} &= E_k + E_g \\ &= \mathrm{1.31\times 10^3\ J + 8.00\times 10^3\ J} \\ &= \mathrm{9.31\times 10^3\ J} \end{align}$$
    2. The cyclist now coasts to the bottom of the hill. How much gravitational potential energy does she have when she reaches the bottom?
      3. $$E_g = mgh = \mathrm{(68.0\ kg) (9.81\ m/s^2) (0.00\ m) = 0.00\ J}$$
    3. Assuming that the cyclist’s mechanical energy did not change (i.e. the gravitational potential energy lost was converted entirely into kinetic energy), what is the cyclist’s speed at the bottom of the hill?
      4. Since there is no gravitational potential energy, the mechanical energy is just the kinetic energy... $$E_k = \mathrm{9.31\times 10^3\ J}$$ Re-arrange the kinetic energy equation... $$E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$ $$\frac{2E_k}{m} = v^2$$ $$v = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m}} = \mathrm{\sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 9.31\times 10^3\ J}{68.0\ kg}} = 16.5\ m/s}$$
  8. A 58.5-gram rock falls from a cliff face. The rock is initially 25.0 metres above the base of the cliff.
    1. Calculate the mechanical energy of the rock before it falls.
      2. Since there is no kinetic energy, the mechanical energy is just the gravitational potential energy... $$E_g = mgh = \mathrm{(0.0585\ kg) (9.81\ m/s^2) (25.0\ m) = 14.3\ J}$$
    2. Calculate the gravitational potential energy of the rock when it is 10.0 metres above the base of the cliff.
      3. $$E_g = mgh = \mathrm{(0.0585\ kg) (9.81\ m/s^2) (10.0\ m) = 5.74\ J}$$
    3. Assuming that the mechanical energy of the rock does not change, how much kinetic energy does it have when it is 10.0 metres above the base of the cliff?
      4. $$\mathrm{Mechanical\ Energy} = E_g + E_k$$ $$E_k = \mathrm{Mechanical\ Energy} - Eg = \mathrm{14.3\ J - 5.74\ J = 8.61\ J}$$
    4. Determine the speed of the rock at this point (i.e. 10.0 metres above the base of the cliff).
      5. The algebra is the same as in #7c... $$v = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m}} = \mathrm{\sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 8.61\ J}{0.0585\ kg}} = 17.2\ m/s}$$
