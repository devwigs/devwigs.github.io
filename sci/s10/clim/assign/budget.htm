Review

  1. Define open, closed and isolated systems. Which type of system is Earth?

    2. Open = Matter and energy can both leave or enter the system.

    Closed = Energy (but not matter) can leave or enter the system.

    Isolated = Neither energy nor mattercan leave or enter the system.

    Earth is nearly a closed system; a very small amount of matter enters or leaves.

  2. What does biosphere mean? What are the three parts of Earth’s biosphere?
    3. Biosphere is the part of Earth where life is found: Atmosphere (air), Hydrosphere (water) and Lithosphere (land).
  3. Why and how is the incoming radiation that Earth receives from the Sun different from the outgoing radiation that Earth sends into space?
    4. The Sun is about 20 times hotter than the Earth, so it emits mostly visible, ultraviolet, and some infrared radiation. Earth emits almost entirely IR.
  4. What do we mean by Earth’s radiation budget? According to the First Law of Thermodynamics, what must happen if the incoming and outgoing radiation do not balance?
    5. This refers to the balance among incoming, reflected, absorbed, and emitted radiation by Earth. According to the Laws of THermodynamics, if there is a difference between the total incoming and outgoing energy, the average temerature of the climate system must change.
  5. Define and explain what albedo means.
    6. Albedo is the percentage of the incoming radiation from the Sun that is reflected without being absorbed.
    1. Describe the greenhouse effect.
      2. Incoming radiation in the Sun is primarily visible light which passes through the atmosphere easily. Outgoing radiation from Earth is infrared, much of which is absorbed by CO2 and other gases in the atmosphere. Since these greenhouse gases affect outgoing but not incoming radiation, they alter the energy budget balance.
    2. How would Earth’s climate be different if there were no greenhouse gases in the atmosphere?
      3. It would be much colder as more infrared radiation would carry energy away from the Earth.
