Review

  1. Describe how Bohr’s model of the atom is similar to and how it is different from Rutherford’s planetary model.
    2. Like Rutherford’s Planetary Model, Bohr’s model has electrons (negative) orbiting around a nucleus made up of protons (positive) and neutrons (neutral). However in the Bohr model, electrons are only permitted to orbit in certain “energy levels”.
  2. How does Bohr’s model explain the “spectrum” of light that we see when we look at a gas discharge tube through a spectroscope?
    3. When an electron emits light by jumping between energy levels, the energy of the light must correspond to the difference between energy levels. Therefore only certain energies (colours) of light can be produced.
  3. How can we use the Periodic Table to remember the maximum number of electrons that each energy level can accommodate?
    4. The number of elements in each period (row) corresponds to the number of electrons that can fit in the corresponding energy level.
  4. What is the maximum number of electrons that can fit in each of these energy levels?
    1. \(n = 1\)
    2. \(n = 2\)
    3. \(n = 3\)
    4. \(n = 4\)
    5. 2, 8, 8, 18
  5. Explain what “valence level” and “valence electrons” mean. Why is this important?
    6. The valence level is the highest energy level that the atom needs to use to accommodate all of its electrons. The number of valence electrons is what determines the chemical properties of the element.
  6. Explain how to draw an “energy level diagram” (also called a Bohr diagram) for any atom or ion using information from the Periodic Table. Use phosphorus-31 as an example.

    7. Draw the nucleus at the bottom and enter the number of protons and neutrons.

    Draw the “energy levels” as lines above the nucleus. The number of energy levels needed is equal to the period (row) of the Periodic Table where the element appears.

    Fill in the electrons in the lower energy levels using the maximums.

    Fill in the electrons in the valence level with the remaining electrons.

    For a neutral atom, the total electrons equal the total protons.

    For an anion (negative) add extra electrons and for a cation (positive) subtract electrons to give the correct total charge.
