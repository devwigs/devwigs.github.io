Velocity

The rate of displacement (i.e. the displacement per second) is called the velocity of the object:$${\vec {\mathbf v}} = {\Delta {\vec {\mathbf d}} \over \Delta t}$$

  • Velocity is a vector because (like displacement!) it has a direction.
  • Velocity is measured in metres per second (m/s).
  • For the bike example from the previous lesson:$${\vec {\mathbf v}} = {\mathrm{{+12.0\ m \over 4.0\ s} = 3.0\ m/s}}$$

Speed

An object’s speed is the distance travelled per second.

  • Speed is a scalar.
  • The equation for speed is identical to velocity but without the arrows:$$v = {\Delta d \over \Delta t}$$
  • Because the distance travelled is often greater than the displacement (unless the motion is by the shortest possible path), the average speed will often be greater than the velocity.

Example

A car travels 300 metres at a speed of 60.0 km/h. How long did the motion take?

Since the time interval is the “unknown”, we need to rearrange the speed formula to isolate \(\Delta t\):

$$v \color{red}{\Delta t} = {\Delta d \over \Delta t} \color{red}{\Delta t}$$

$$v \Delta t = \Delta d$$

$${v \Delta t \over \color{red}{v}} = {\Delta d \over \color{red}{v}}$$

$$\Delta t = {\Delta d \over v}$$

  • We can’t “mix” different units (i.e. \(\mathrm{m}\) and \(\mathrm{km}\)).

$$\Delta t = \mathrm {{0.300\ km \over 60.0\ km/h} = 0.00500\ h}$$

$$\Delta t = \mathrm {{300\ m \over 16.\overline{6}\ m/s} = 18.0\ s}$$
