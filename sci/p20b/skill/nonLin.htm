If your hypothesis predicts an equation in which the variables (manipulated and/or responding) appear as a power:

  • Use the exponent from the equation to transform the data.
  • If a variable appears in the denominator of a fraction, treat the exponent as negative.
  • If a variable appears under a radical sign, treat the exponent as a fraction (1/2 for square root, 1/3 for cube root).
  • If the hypothesis is correct, transforming the data will make the graph linear, allowing you to add a best-fit line.
  • Calculate the slope and intercept as you would for any line.
  • When writing the model equation, use the transformed variable (i.e. the power) rather than the “raw” variable.

If you do not have a hypothesis that predicts an equation:

  • Use a graphing calculator or statistics software to perform a power regression.
  • Graphing the data and equation (on the calclator or computer) to see how well it fits your data.
  • If power regression does not fit, you can try exponential, quadratic, polynomial, or other regressions.
