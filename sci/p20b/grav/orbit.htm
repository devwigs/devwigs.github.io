Kepler’s 3rd Law, Again

Let’s reconsider the case of a mass (\(m\)) orbiting in uniform circular motion around a very large central mass (\(M\)).

  • We know for uniform circular motion that the orbiting object’s acceleration is directed toward the centre of its circular orbit and has a magnitude of $$a_c=\frac{v^2}{r}=\frac{4\pi^2r}{T^2}$$
  • If the central mass is much larger than any other nearby mass, then the gravitational field will be dominated by the central mass’s contribution: $$|\vec{\mathbf{g}}|=\frac{GM}{r^2}$$
  • If there are no forces other than gravity, we know that \(\vec{\mathbf{a}}=\vec{\mathbf{g}}\)!
  • This would not be the case if, for example, the orbiting mass was moving under rocket thrust, or if it was moving through an atmosphere.
  • By substituting the equations for \(\vec{\mathbf{a}}\) and \(\vec{\mathbf{g}}\) we get: $$\frac{4\pi^2r}{T^2}=\frac{GM}{r^2}$$
  • We can rearrange this as: $$4\pi^2r^3=GMT^2$$
  • This is Kepler’s 3rd Law, expressed in terms of the central mass rather than the “Kepler coefficient” (\(k\))!

Planetary & Stellar Masses

This equation gives us a means of calculating masses (\(M\)) for stars and planets by measuring the orbital radius (\(r\)) and period (\(T\)) of objects in orbit.

  • We can determine Earth’s mass using the radius and period of the Moon’s orbit.
  • We can determine the Sun’s mass from the radius and period of Earth’s orbit.

Geosynchronous Orbits

When a satellite has the same period as the planet’s rotation (e.g. 24 hours for Earth), the orbit is called geosynchronous.

  • A satellite in a geosynchronous orbit passes over the same point on the ground at the same time each day.
  • A circular, geosynchronous orbit around the equator is called geostationary.
  • The satellite remains in orbit permanently above the same point on the ground.
