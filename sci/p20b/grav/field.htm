Action at a Distance

Newton’s equation for gravitational force implies that the force between masses can act over large distances.

  • Each mass somehow “knows” the position and the motion of all of the other distant masses that affect it gravitationally.
  • Scientists found this action at a distance to be a very complicated way of running the universe and sought a simpler explanation of the gravitational attraction.

Gravitational Field

They devised the concept of a gravitational field...

  • The idea of a field is that every mass causes the space around it to become distorted, and each mass moves within the distorted space that it finds itself in.
  • As an analogy, think of two bowling balls placed on a trampoline...
  • The bowling balls will roll toward each other, as if there were some force of attraction between them.
  • What really happens is that the bowling balls change the shape of the trampoline, and each ball rolls according to the shape of the trampoline in its own location.
  • If we place a large number of bowling balls, baseballs, and golf balls on the trampoline, each one will affect the shape of the surface; the bowling balls will have more effect than the golf balls!
  • The shape of the trampoline will become very complicated and each ball will roll in a complicated trajectory.
  • However, in this model none of the various objects needs to know where any of the other objects is located.

Sources & Test Masses

To measure the shape of the trampoline at any particular spot, as a result of all the masses on the trampoline, we can observe a small object at that spot, say a ping-pong ball which is light enough to have negligible effect on the trampoline’s shape. The ping-pong ball’s acceleration will tell us how steeply and in what direction the trampoline slopes.

  • If we can think of space as a 3D trampoline, the gravitational field represents its “shape”.
  • We measure the gravitational field by placing a tiny test mass at some point in space, and by observing its acceleration when there are no forces except gravity acting on it.
  • The masses that create the gravitational field are known as the field masses, or the sources of the field.

Equations

The gravitational field due to a single source can be calculated from Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation and Newton’s 2nd Law.

  • The force that the single source (\(M\)) causes on the test mass (\(m\)), is: $$|{\vec{\mathbf{F}}}_g|=\frac{GMm}{r^2}$$
  • According to Newton’s 2nd Law, to calculate the field (acceleration of the test mass), we need to divide the force by the test mass: $$|\vec{\mathbf{g}}|=\frac{|{\vec{\mathbf{F}}}_g|}{m}=\frac{GM}{r^2}$$
  • The vector \(\vec{\mathbf{g}}\) is the gravitational field.
  • The gravitational field vector and the test mass acceleration \(\vec{\mathbf{a}}\) are the same provided that gravity is the only force present.
  • When there are other forces, \(\vec{\mathbf{a}}\) will not equal \(\vec{\mathbf{g}}\).
  • If there is more than one source present, we can use the above expression, \(GM/r^2\), to calculate each source’s effect on the test mass, and then determine the net gravitational field (i.e. the test mass’s acceleration) by doing a vector addition of all sources’ contributions to the field.
