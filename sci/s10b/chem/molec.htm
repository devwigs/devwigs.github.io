Compounds are described in Chapter 2 (Names, Formulas and Properties) of your Science 10 textbook.

Chemical Bonds

Chemical bonds are the forces that hold atoms together as molecules (H2O) or crystal lattices (NaCl).

  • Atoms prefer to have filled valence levels.
  • They can achieve this by gaining, losing, or sharing electrons.
  • Bonds between atoms that share electrons are called covalent bonds..

Covalent Bonds

  • Chlorine atoms have seven valence electrons.
  • Each chlorine atom needs to gain an electron to fill its valence.
  • Two chlorine atoms can form a covalent bond by sharing one electron with each other.

  • A shared pair of electrons is illustrated on a dot diagram by a line.
  • The shared pair count as valence electrons for both atoms.
  • A chlorine molecule (\(\mathrm{Cl}_2\)) consists of two chlorine atoms held together by their attraction to a shared pair of electrons.
  • This is how covalent bonds work.

Molecular Elements

Elements like chlorine that form molecules with two atoms are called diatomic elements.

  • There are eight diatomic elements.
  • We can remember which ones using the 7+1 Rule.

  • Seven elements in the shape of a 7, starting with atomic number 7...
  • ...and atomic number 1.
  • The elements under the top of the 7 are polyatomic: P4 and S8.

Molecular Compounds

Molecular compounds are compounds formed by covalent bonding between two or more elements to the right of the staircase.

  • In Science 10, we do not have to draw dot diagrams or figure out the shape of molecular compounds.

Naming Molecular Compounds

  • For molecular compounds formed from two elements, we name the element furthest left on the periodic table first, then the second one with the suffix -ide.
  • We use prefixes (mono-, di-, tri-, tetra-, penta-) to indicate how many of each type of atom are present in each molecule.
  • \(\mathrm{P_2O_5}\) is called diphosphorous pentaoxide.
  • \(\mathrm{H_2O}\) is called dihydrogen monoxide.
  • \(\mathrm{CCl_4}\) is called carbon tetrachloride.
  • Omit the prefix mono- on the first element.

Alternate Names

Some molecular compounds have common names that can be used as an alternative to their systematic names.

  • Water is \(\mathrm{H_2O}\).
  • Ammonia is \(\mathrm{NH_3}\).
  • Propane is \(\mathrm{C_3H_8}\).
  • Ethanol is \(\mathrm{C_2H_6O}\) (or \(\mathrm{C_2H_5OH}\)).
  • Glucose is \(\mathrm{C_6H_{12}O_6}\).
