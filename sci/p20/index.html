Equilibrium

An equilibrium is a position where the forces acting on an object balance, so that the acceleration would be zero.

An equilibrium is stable when an object close to equilibrium experiences a restoring force causing it to accelerate toward the equilibrium position.

Objects close to a stable equilibrium will oscillate: move back and forth across the equilibrium.

The time for one complete oscillation, before the motion begins to repeat, is called the period, (\(T\)).

The reciprocal of the period is called the frequency and is measured in hertz (Hz, oscillations per second): $$f = {1\over T}$$

Simple Harmonic Motion

Simple harmonic motion (SHM) is a type of oscillation that occurs when the restoring force is directly proportional to the object‚Äôs displacement from equilibrium, \(\vec{\mathbf x}\): $$\vec{\mathbf F}_{net} = -k\vec{\mathbf x}$$

When this is the case, graphs of the object‚Äôs position, velocity, and acceleration (all versus time) will all be shaped like a sine or cosine function.

All three graphs have the same period.

The starting points (y-intercepts) of the graphs are shifted...

  • \(\vec{\mathbf v}\) is one-quarter period ‚Äúout of phase‚Äù with \(\vec{\mathbf x}\) because the kinetic energy must be at its maximum when potential energy is at its minimum (and vice versa).
  • \(\vec{\mathbf a}\) is one-quarter period out of phase with \(\vec{\mathbf v}\), and one-half period out of phase with \(\vec{\mathbf x}\).

The vertical scales of the graphs are all different, but are related: $$v_{max} = {2\pi A\over T}$$ $$a_{max} = {v_{max}^2 \over A} = {4\pi^2 A\over T^2} = {2\pi v_{max}\over T}$$

  • The symbol \(A\) is called the amplitude and it represents \(x_{max}\), the maximum distance the object moves from its equilibrium.

Period of SHM / Hooke‚Äôs Law

By substituting the above formulas into Newton‚Äôs 2nd Law, we can obtain an equation for the period: $$T = 2\pi\sqrt{m\over k}$$

Hooke‚Äôs Law states that the elastic force exerted by a stretch or compressed object will follow the equation below, within a range of \(\Delta L\) values: $$\vec{\mathbf F}_{elas} = -k\Delta L$$

If the elastic force is the only force, or it combines with a constant force such as gravity, the conditions for SHM will be met as long as the elastic remains within its elastic limit.

Uniform Circular Motion

Uniform circular motion means motion with constant speed along a circular path.

The time for one complete ‚Äúorbit‚Äù is called the period, \(T\). Period is related to the speed and radius of the motion:

$$v = {2\pi r \over T}$$

The reciprocal of the period is called the frequency. Frequency is measured in hertz (Hz):

$$f = {1\over T}$$

The velocity for UCM is in the tangential direction (along the circumference of the circle). Velocity is not constant because its direction is constantly changing.

Centripetal Acceleration

Changing direction requires a force (and therefore an acceleration) according to Newton‚Äôs Laws.

For the speed to remain constant, the work done by the force must be exactly zero and the force must be perpendicular to the velocity.

The net force for UCM is directed exactly toward the centre of the circle. This is called the centrieptal direction and the acceleration and net force are often called the centripetal acceleration and centripetal force.

Centripetal acceleration can be calculated as follows:

$$a_c = {v^2 \over r} = {4\pi^2 r\over T^2}$$

Planetary Motion

Copernicus developed the heliocentric model of the Solar System (circular orbits centred around the Sun) to better explain observations like the retrograde motion of Mars.

Using Brahe‚Äôs extensive data, Kepler concluded that planetary orbits are actually elliptical. Kepler's Laws of Planetary Motion are:

  1. Orbits are ellipse-shaped with the Sun located at the focus (an off-centre point on the major-axis).
  2. The areas formed by the Sun and the orbit will always be equal in equal amounts of time.
  3. There is a mathematical relationship between the mean orbital radius (semi-major axis) and oribtal period: $$T^2 = kr^3$$ $$\left({T_2\over T_1}\right)^2 = \left({r_2\over r_1}\right)^3$$
