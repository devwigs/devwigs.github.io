Practice

  1. A hiker moving in 1D has an initial position of –4.0 km and a final position of +3.2 km relative to some arbitrary origin.
    1. Draw a vector diagram of the hiker’s motion. Label \(\vec{\mathbf d}_i\) (the initial position), \(\vec{\mathbf d}_f\) (the final position), and \(\Delta\vec{\mathbf d}\) (the displacement).
    2. Calculate the hiker’s displacement. Show your work.
      $$\Delta\vec{\mathbf d} = \vec{\mathbf d}_f - \vec{\mathbf d}_i = \mathrm{(+3.2 - (-4.0))\ km = +7.2\ km}$$
    3. The hiker now walks to a third position of +1.0 km. Draw and label a new vector diagram showing both parts of the motion and the resultant displacement.
    4. Calculate the resultant displacement and the total distance the hiker walked.

      $$\Delta\vec{\mathbf d} = \vec{\mathbf d}_f - \vec{\mathbf d}_i = \mathrm{(+1.0 - (-4.0))\ km = +5.0\ km}$$

      Alternatively, you could calculate the second part of the displacement: \(\Delta\vec{\mathbf d}_2 = \mathrm{(+1.0 - 3.2)\ km = -2.2\ km}\), and then add thtis to the first displacement (\(\mathrm{+7.2\ km}\)) to get the same answer.

  2. A hiker walks 6.00 km north and then 5.00 km east. By drawing an accurate vector diagram of the motion, estimate the magnitude of the hiker’s displacement. Can you think of a way to calculate the magnitude of the displacement?

    Draw an accurate “tip-to-tail” diagram as shown by the red arrows below. The scale of the diagram is 1 square = 0.5 km. The blue arrow is the total displacment.

    You can measaure the magnitude of the displacment (the length of the blue arrow) using the scale of the diagram, or you can calculate it using the Pythagoras equation.

  3. A student rides her bicycle 5.00 km north in 12.0 min. After a 5.0 min rest, she rides back south to her starting point in 15.0 min. Determine the student’s displacement and distance traveled for the entire trip.

    The displacment is zero because her final position is the same as her initial position.

    You can show this by adding the two displacement vectors: $$\Delta\vec{\mathbf d} = \Delta\vec{\mathbf d}_1 + \Delta\vec{\mathbf d}_2 = \mathrm{+5.00\ km + (-5.00\ km) = 0.00\ km}$$

    Distance is not a vector, so we ignore the direction: $$\Delta d = |\Delta\vec{\mathbf d}_1| + |\Delta\vec{\mathbf d}_2| = \mathrm{5.00\ km + 5.00\ km = 10.0\ km}$$
