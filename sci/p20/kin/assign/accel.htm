Practice

  1. A car accelerates from 50.0 km/h to 80.0 km/h in 4.00 seconds. Calculate the car’s acceleration. [Important: km/h is not the standard SI unit of velocity!]
    2. Given: $${\vec{\mathbf v}}_i = \mathrm{50.0\ km/h = 50.0 \frac{1000\ m}{3600\ s} = 13.9\ m/s}$$ $${\vec{\mathbf v}}_f = \mathrm{80.0\ km/h = 80.0 \frac{1000\ m}{3600\ s} = 22.2\ m/s}$$ $$\Delta t = \mathrm{4.00\ s}$$ Solution: $$\vec{\mathbf a} = \frac{\Delta{\vec{\mathbf v}}}{\Delta t} = \mathrm{\frac{22.2\ m/s - 13.9\ m/s}{4.00 s} = +2.08\ m/s^2}$$
  2. An apple falls from a tree, beginning from rest and striking the ground with a downward velocity of 5.00 m/s.
    1. Assuming that gravity is solely responsible for the apple’s acceleration, how long did it take the apple to fall?
      2. Given: $${\vec{\mathbf v}}_i = \mathrm{0.00\ m/s}$$ $${\vec{\mathbf v}}_f = \mathrm{-5.00\ m/s}$$ $$\vec{\mathbf a} = \mathrm{-9.81\ m/s^2}$$ Solution: Rearrange the acceleration equation to isolate \(\Delta t\)... $$\vec{\mathbf a} = \frac{\Delta{\vec{\mathbf v}}}{\Delta t}$$ $$\vec{\mathbf a}\Delta t = {\vec{\mathbf v}}_f - {\vec{\mathbf v}}_i$$ $$\Delta t = \frac{{\vec{\mathbf v}}_f - {\vec{\mathbf v}}_i}{\vec{\mathbf a}} = \mathrm{\frac{-5.00\ m/s - 0.00\ m/s}{-9.81\ m/s^2} = 0.510\ s}$$
    2. When the effects of air resistance are considered, would the acceleration be larger or smaller? Would the final velocity be faster or slower? Would the time increase or decrease? Justify your answers.
      3. Air resistance causes the apple to lose some of its energy so it will fall more slowly. This means the time will be larger and the final velocity and acceleration will be smaller.
  3. Traffic radar recorded data for a speeding car. A graph of the data is shown below.

    Motion of a Speeding Car: \(\vec{\mathbf{v}}\) vs. \(t\)

    1. At what time did the speeding car begin to slow down?
      2. According to the graph, the velocity remained constant for the first 3.0 seconds and then the car began to decelerate (i.e. accelerate in the negative direction).
    2. What was the car’s acceleration for the time interval over which it was slowing?
      3. Given: $${\vec{\mathbf v}}_i = \mathrm{125\ km/h = 125 \frac{1000\ m}{3600\ s} = 34.7\ m/s}$$ $${\vec{\mathbf v}}_f = \mathrm{0.00\ m/s}$$ $$\Delta t = \mathrm{8.00\ s - 3.00\ s = 5.00\ s}$$ Solution: $$\vec{\mathbf a} = \frac{\Delta{\vec{\mathbf v}}}{\Delta t} = \mathrm{\frac{0.00\ m/s - 34.7\ m/s}{5.00 s} = -6.94\ m/s^2}$$
    3. What was the car’s average acceleration for the entire 8.0-second time?
      4. Given: $${\vec{\mathbf v}}_i = \mathrm{34.7\ m/s}$$ $${\vec{\mathbf v}}_f = \mathrm{0.00\ m/s}$$ $$\Delta t = \mathrm{8.00\ s}$$ Solution: $$\vec{\mathbf a} = \frac{\Delta{\vec{\mathbf v}}}{\Delta t} = \mathrm{\frac{0.00\ m/s - 34.7\ m/s}{8.00 s} = -4.34\ m/s^2}$$
    4. Calculate the displacement of the car over the 8.0 seconds of the motion.
      5. Rearrange the velocity equation to isolate \(\Delta \vec{\mathbf d}\)... $$\vec{\mathbf v} = \frac{\Delta\vec{\mathbf d}}{\Delta t}$$ $$\Delta\vec{\mathbf d} = \vec{\mathbf v}\Delta t$$ For the first 3.00 seconds (uniform motion): $$\Delta\vec{\mathbf d} = \mathrm{34.7\ m/s\cdot 3.00\ s = 104\ m}$$ For the next 5.00 seconds (accelerated motion), use the average velocity: $$\Delta\vec{\mathbf d} = \mathrm{\frac{34.7\ m/s + 0.00\ m/s}{2}\cdot 5.00\ s = 86.8\ m}$$ The total displacement for both parts of the motion combined is 191 m.
  4. A cyclist riding at 9.20 m/s applies her brakes, causing and acceleration of –1.20 m/s2.
    1. Explain the significance of the acceleration being negative.
      2. This means that the velocity is decreasing.
    2. By rearranging the acceleration formula, calculate the time it takes for the cyclist to come to rest.
      3. Given: $${\vec{\mathbf v}}_i = \mathrm{9.20\ m/s}$$ $${\vec{\mathbf v}}_f = \mathrm{0.00\ m/s}$$ $$\vec{\mathbf a} = \mathrm{-1.20\ m/s^2}$$ Solution: Rearrange the acceleration equation to isolate \(\Delta t\)... $$\vec{\mathbf a} = \frac{\Delta{\vec{\mathbf v}}}{\Delta t}$$ $$\vec{\mathbf a}\Delta t = {\vec{\mathbf v}}_f - {\vec{\mathbf v}}_i$$ $$\Delta t = \frac{{\vec{\mathbf v}}_f - {\vec{\mathbf v}}_i}{\vec{\mathbf a}} = \mathrm{\frac{0.00\ m/s - 9.20\ m/s}{-1.20\ m/s^2} = 7.67\ s}$$
    3. Graph the cyclist’s velocity versus time. Use the graph paper on the back page.

      4. The velocity is a straight line from (0.00 s, 9.20 m/s) to (7.67 s, 0.00 m/s). Alternatively, you can make a table of values by starting at 9.20 m/s at \(t\) = 0 and decreasing by 1.20 m/s every second.

      Graph

    4. Calculate the cyclist’s instantaneous velocity 2.00 seconds after she began braking. (Find what \({\vec{\mathbf{v}}}_f\) becomes when you change \(\Delta t\) to 2.00 s.) Is your answer in agreement with your graph?
      5. Given: $${\vec{\mathbf v}}_i = \mathrm{9.20\ m/s}$$ $$\vec{\mathbf a} = \mathrm{-1.20\ m/s^2}$$ $$\Delta t = \mathrm{2.00\ s}$$ Solution: Rearrange the acceleration equation to isolate \({\vec{\mathbf v}}_f\)... $$\vec{\mathbf a} = \frac{\Delta{\vec{\mathbf v}}}{\Delta t}$$ $$\vec{\mathbf a}\Delta t = {\vec{\mathbf v}}_f - {\vec{\mathbf v}}_i$$ $${\vec{\mathbf v}}_f = \vec{\mathbf a}\Delta t + {\vec{\mathbf v}}_i = \mathrm{-1.20\ m/s^2\cdot 2.00\ s + 9.20\ m/s = 6.80\ m/s}$$
    5. Given that the cyclist accelerated uniformly from 9.20 m/s to 0.00 m/s, estimate the average velocity of the cyclist over the entire motion. Use the result to calculate her displacement while braking.
      6. The average velocity is likely the arithmetic average of the initial and final velocities: $${\vec{\mathbf v}}_{avg} = \frac{{\vec{\mathbf v}}_i + {\vec{\mathbf v}}_f}{2} = \mathrm{\frac{9.20\ m/s + 0.00\ m/s}{2} = 4.60\ m/s}$$ The displacement (as in #3d) is: $$\Delta\vec{\mathbf d} = \vec{\mathbf v}\Delta t = \mathrm{4.60\ m/s\cdot 7.67\ s = 35.3\ m}$$
    6. Calculate the area of the triangle formed by the line on your graph and the axes. Compare this area with the displacement you calculated in (e).
      7. $$\mathrm{Area = \frac{Base\times Height}{2} = \frac{7.67\ s\times 9.20\ m/s}{2} = 35.3\ m}$$
