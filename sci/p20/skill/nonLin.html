Transforming Power Data

Suppose our hypothesis is an equation (from our Data Sheet?) that predicts the relationship between manipulated and responding variables: $$g=\frac{GM}{r^2}$$

  • Variables that appear in the denominator are considered to have a negative exponent: $$g=GMr^{-2}$$
  • Any equation of the form \(y = ax^n\) is classified as a power relationship.
  • The responding variable, \(g\), does not have an exponent so it does not need to be transformed.
  • We can transform the manipulated variable by calculating \(r^{-2}\).
  • We do this because \(-2\) is the exponent that appears with \(r\) in the equation!
  • The first two points in the data table are...

$$\mathrm{(6.37\times 10^{-6}\ m)^{-2} = 2.46\times 10^{-14}\ m^{-2}}$$

$$\mathrm{(7.00\times 10^{-6}\ m)^{-2} = 2.04\times 10^{-14}\ m^{-2}}$$

  • If the data does follow a power relationship and we have used the correct exponent, \(-2\) in this case, the transformed data will now be linear!

Model Equation

Now that the graph has been linearized, we can calculate its slope and \(y\)-intercept as with any other line.

$$b = \mathrm{0.0\ m/s^2}$$

$$k = \mathrm{\frac{(10.0 - 0.0)\ m/s^2}{(2.5 - 0.0) \times 10^{-14}\ m^{-2}} = 4.0 \times 10^{14}\ m^3/s^2}$$

  • When you write the model equation, \(y=kx+b\), remember to include the power: $$g = \mathrm{(4.0 \times 10^{14}\ m^3/s^2)}\ r^{-2}$$
  • The \(y\)-variable is \(g\); what was graphed on the \(y\)-axis.
  • The \(x\)-variable is \(r^{-2}\); what was graphed on the \(x\)-axis.
  • You can substitute the model equation into the theoretical equation: $$g=\frac{GM}{r^2} = \mathrm{(4.0 \times 10^{14}\ m^3/s^2)}\ r^{-2}$$
  • You could then algebraically solve for \(M\), if you know \(G\).

Example 1

Students conducted an experiment on sound waves to test the equation \(v=f\lambda\). They manipulated the frequency of the waves \((f)\) and controlled the speed \((v)\). The wavelength \((\lambda)\) was the responding variable. How should they transform the data?

Isolate the responding variable:$$\lambda = \frac{v}{f}$$

Since \(f\) appears in the denominator, use a negative power:$$\lambda = vf^{-1}$$

Transform the data by calculating \(f^{-1}\) for each data point.

Example 2

Students conducted an experiment on a simple pendulum to see how the length \((L)\) of the pendulum affected its period \((T)\), the time for one swing. Their hypothesis was: $$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$ How should they transform the data?

The square root sign is equivalent to a power of \(\frac{1}{2}\): $$T = 2\pi\frac{L^{\frac{1}{2}}}{g^{\frac{1}{2}}}$$

Transform the data by calculating \(L^{\frac{1}{2}}\) for each data point.

  • Since \(g\) is a controlled variable, it will not be graphed, so it doesn’t matter that it has an exponent!
  • Alternatively, we could get rid of the square root sign by squaring the original equation: $$T^2 = 4\pi^2\frac{L}{g}$$
  • This alternative says we can transform the data by calculating \(T^2\), while not transforming \(L\).
