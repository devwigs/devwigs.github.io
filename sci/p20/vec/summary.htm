Vectors are quantities that involve a direction, such as displacement, velocity, acceleration, and force.

  • We can illustrate vectors using a vector diagram, in which each vector is drawn as an arrow illustrating the direction and the magnitude (size) of the vector.
  • To illustrate the addition of two or more vectors, the vectors are drawn in a tip-to-tail fashion.

Cartesian Form means that a 2D vector is described as separate \(x\)- and \(y\)-components; \(\Delta \vec{\mathbf d}_1 = \mathrm{(5.0, 8.7)\ m}\).

Polar Form describes a vector by its magnitude and its direction; \(\Delta \vec{\mathbf d}_1 = \mathrm{10\ m\ [60°]}\).

  • The direction is an angle measured counter-clockwise from the \(+\)x-direction.
  • We can convert a vector from polar to Cartesian form: $${\vec{\mathbf{v}}}_x=\left|\vec{\mathbf{v}}\right|\cos{\theta}$$ $${\vec{\mathbf{v}}}_y=\left|\vec{\mathbf{v}}\right|\sin{\theta}$$
  • We can also convert a vector from Cartesian to polar form: $$\left|\vec{\mathbf{v}}\right|=\sqrt{v_x^2+v_y^2}$$ $$\tan{\theta}=\frac{{\vec{\mathbf{v}}}_y}{{\vec{\mathbf{v}}}_x}$$
  • To isolate \(\theta\), you need to apply \(\tan^{–1}\) to the last equation; this will give only one of two possible answers.
  • If the \(x\)-component of a vector is negative (if the vector points into the second or third quadrant), you will need to add 180° to find the second (correct) solution for \(\theta\).
  • When performing arithmetic or algebra involving vector quantities, treat the \(x\)- and \(y\)-components independently; perform your calculations on the \(x\)-components alone, and then on the \(y\)-components alone. (Some problems may require you to solve the \(y\)-component first.)
  • Alternatively, some 2D vector problems can be solved by applying trigonometric relationships to the vector diagram.
  • Remember that the Pythagoras equation and “SOH CAH TOA” apply only to triangles containing a right angle. For triangles that don’t (or may not) have a 90° angle, you can use the Law of Cosines and Law of Sines instead.

Projectile Motion is the 2D version of uniform accelerated motion.

  • Gravity causes a constant acceleration in the \(-y\) direction; thus we can use the UAM equations to solve for the \(y\)-components of the kinematics vectors.
  • There are no forces in the \(x\)-direction, so the \(x\)-motion is simply uniform motion (constant velocity).
  • The hang time means the total time it takes the projectile to first land on the ground.
  • The range means the hortizontal displacment of the projectile when it first lands on the ground.
