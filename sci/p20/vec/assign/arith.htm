Review

  1. Which form of 2D vector (polar or Cartesian) is more convenient when adding or subtracting vectors?
    2. Cartesian
  2. What do we mean by the resultant vector?
    3. The total of the vectors being added.
  3. Explain how to add vectors using each of these methods: (a) graphical method, (b) component method, (c) trigonometry method.
    • In the graphical method, draw the vectors accurately — tip-to-tail! — on graph paper using a protractor and ruler. Use the same scale for both axes. Draw the resultant vector (preferably in a different colour) as an arrow from the tail of the first vector to the tip of the last vector. The order in which vectors are added/drawn does not affect the resultant. Label all vectors. Measure the direction (angle) and magnitude (length of the arrow, based on the scale of the diagram) for the resultant vector.
    • In the component method, write all vectors in Cartesian form. Use the \(\sin\) and \(\cos\) functions to convert polar vectors to Cartesian form. Next add the \(x\)-components together to the get the \(x\)-component of the resultant. Now repeat for the \(y\)-components. If necessary, convert the resultant vector back to polar form using the Pythagoras and \(\tan^{-1}\) equations. This is the most precise and reliable method of vector addition!
    • Draw a sketch of the (tip-to-tail) vector diagram. Label all known sides and angles. Use the Pythagoras and/or \(\sin\), \(\cos\), or \(\tan\) equations to solve the triangle only if it must contain a 90° angle. Otherwise use the Sine Law and/or Cosine Law. This method can only be used to add two vectors at a time.
  4. Explain how to subtract vectors.
    5. To subtract a vector, add its negative! In Cartesian form, reverse the signs of both the \(x\)- and \(y\)-components. In polar form, add or subtract 180° from the direction.

    1. Express Kelly and Sam’s force in Cartesian form and use the results to calculate the net force acting on the backpack.

      2. For Kelly... $$\begin{align} \vec{\mathbf F}_x &= |\vec{\mathbf F}|\cos\theta \\ &= \mathrm{82.0\ N\cdot \cos(+25.0^\circ)} \\ &= \mathrm{+74.3\ N} \end{align}$$ $$\begin{align} \vec{\mathbf F}_y &= |\vec{\mathbf F}|\sin\theta \\ &= \mathrm{82.0\ N\cdot \sin(+25.0^\circ)} \\ &= \mathrm{+34.7\ N} \end{align}$$

  4. A student can paddle a canoe with a speed of 5.00 m/s. The student wants to cross a stream that has a current of 1.85 m/s. The actual velocity of the student will be the vector sum of the paddling velocity added to the current velocity. Determine the direction that the student should paddle (i.e. how many degrees upstream) so that the resultant velocity will be straight across the stream (i.e. neither upstream nor downstream.) Also, determine the magnitude of the resultant velocity. Take the current direction to be the x-axis and the direction of the resultant velocity to be the y-axis.

    5. Vector Diagram

    Since we know the magnitudes of the current and paddling velocity, we can calculate the magnitude of the resultant velocity using Pythagoras: $$\vec{\mathbf v}_{net} = \mathrm{\sqrt{(5.00\ m/s)^2 - (1.85\ m/s)^2} = 4.65\ m/s}$$ We can now calculate the paddling velocty in Cartesian form: $$\begin{align} \vec{\mathbf v}_{paddle} &= \vec{\mathbf v}_{net} – \vec{\mathbf v}_{current} \\ &= \mathrm{(0.00, +4.65)\ m/s - (1.85, 0.00)\ m/s} \\ &= \mathrm{(-1.85, +4.65)\ m/s} \end{align}$$

    The direction of the paddling velocity is: $$\theta = \mathrm{\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{+4.65}{-1.85}\right) + 180^\circ = 112^\circ}$$ (i.e. 22° upstream from straight across)

  5. A spacecraft moves between a planet and its moon as illustrated below. The observed acceleration of the spacecraft due to the combined gravity of the planet and moon has a direction of +270º. Mission Control calculates that the gravity due to the planet only is 0.462 m/s2 [+230º]. The moon’s direction relative to the spacecraft is +300º, however the moon’s mass is unknown so Mission Control cannot calculate its gravitational field strength.
    1. Sketch and label a vector diagram (“tip-to-tail”) of the two gravitational fields and the resultant (net) gravitational field.
      2. Vector Diagram
    2. Solve for all unknowns in the table below.
      3. Object\(|\vec{\mathbf g}|\)\(\theta\)\(\vec{\mathbf g}_x\)\(\vec{\mathbf g}_y\)
      Planet0.462230°
      Moon300°
      Net270°

      Convert the planet’s gravity to Cartesian form by multiplying 0.462 m/s2 by the cosine and the sine of 230°: $$\vec{\mathbf g}_x = \mathrm{0.462\ m/s^2\cdot\cos(230^\circ) = -0.297\ m/s^2}$$ $$\vec{\mathbf g}_y = \mathrm{0.462\ m/s^2\cdot\sin(230^\circ) = -0.354\ m/s^2}$$

      We know that the net gravity has an x-component of exactly zero because the direction is 270°, so the x-component for the moon must be the exact opposite of the planet’s...

      Object\(|\vec{\mathbf g}|\)\(\theta\)\(\vec{\mathbf g}_x\)\(\vec{\mathbf g}_y\)
      Planet0.462230°-0.297-0.354
      Moon300°+0.297

      Next, solve for the magnitude of the moon’s gravity and then its y-component: $$\vec{\mathbf g}_x = |\vec{\mathbf g}|\cos\theta$$ $$|\vec{\mathbf g}| = \frac{\vec{\mathbf g}_x}{\cos\theta} = \mathrm{\frac{+0.297\ m/s^2}{\cos(300^\circ)} = 0.594\ m/s^2}$$ $$\vec{\mathbf g}_y = |\vec{\mathbf g}|\sin\theta = \mathrm{0.594\ m/s^2\cdot\sin(300^\circ) = -0.514\ m/s^2}$$

      Finally, add the y-components to determine the resultant gravity: $$\Sigma\vec{\mathbf g}_y = \mathrm{-0.354\ m/s^2 + (-0.514\ m/s^2) = -0.868\ m/s^2}$$
