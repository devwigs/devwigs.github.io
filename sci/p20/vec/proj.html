Projectile Motion

Projectile motion is the 2D version of uniformly accelerated motion.

  • This type of motion typically occurs when gravity causes a constant acceleration (if we ignore air resistance) in the \(–y\) direction while the object’s velocity has both \(x\)- and \(y\)-components.
  • The shape of a projectile’s trajectory is a parabola.
  • We know this because the relationship between the \(x\)- and \(y\)-components of the displacement is quadratic.
  • (You will study quadratic functions in Math 20.)
  • For a typical projectile problem, you will express all four of your kinematic vectors (acceleration, initial and final velocity, displacement) in Cartesian form.
  • To solve for any unknowns, treat the \(x\) and \(y\)-parts of the motions as two separate problems to be solved independently.

Example

A golfer imparts an initial velocity of 40.0 m/s [52.0°] to a ball. The ball lands on the green 3.00 metres lower than the tee area. Calculate the “hang time” and “range” of the ball.

  • Hang time means the time the projectile is in motion before it contacts the ground.
  • Range means the horizontal displacement of the projectile before it hits the ground.
  • Since there is no acceleration in the \(x\)-direction, the intial and final velocities are the same.
  • Since time is a scalar, it does not have separate \(x\)- and \(y\)-components.
 \(x\)\(y\)
\(\vec{\mathbf a}\) / \((\mathrm{m/s^2})\)0–9.81
\(\vec{\mathbf v}_i\) / \((\mathrm{m/s})\)24.631.5
\(\vec{\mathbf v}_f\) / \((\mathrm{m/s})\)–32.4
\(\Delta\vec{\mathbf d}\) / \((\mathrm{m})\)161–3.00
\(\Delta t\) / \((\mathrm{s})\)6.52

Solve the \(y\)-motion as uniform accelerated motion. First find the final velocity...

$$v_f^2 = v_i^2 + 2\vec{\mathbf a}\cdot\Delta\vec{\mathbf d}$$

$$v_f = \sqrt{v_i^2 + 2\vec{\mathbf a}\cdot\Delta\vec{\mathbf d}}$$

$$v_f = \sqrt{\mathrm{(31.5\ m/s)^2 + 2(-9.81\ m/s^2)(-3.00\ m)}}$$

$$\vec{\mathbf v}_f = \mathrm{-32.4\ m/s}$$

Next find the time...

$$\vec{\mathbf a} = {\vec{\mathbf v}_f - \vec{\mathbf v}_i \over \Delta t}$$

$$\Delta t = {\vec{\mathbf v}_f - \vec{\mathbf v}_i \over \vec{\mathbf a}}$$

$$\Delta t = \mathrm{{-32.4\ m/s - 31.5\ m/s \over -9.81\ m/s^2} = 6.52\ s}$$

Now solve the \(x\)-motion as uniform motion...

$$\Delta\vec{\mathbf d} = \vec{\mathbf v}\Delta t = \mathrm{24.6\ m/s\cdot 6.52\ s = 161\ m}$$
