Uniform Circular Motion is motion along a circular path with a constant speed: $$v=\frac{2\pi r}{T}$$

  • The velocity is not constant because the direction is continuously changing.
  • UCM occurs when a force acts toward the centre of the circular path to produce a centripetal acceleration: $$a_c=\frac{2\pi v}{T}=\frac{v^2}{r}=\frac{4\pi^2r}{T^2}$$
  • The adjective centripetal means “toward the centre”.

Amusement park rides often use circular motion to produce a sensation of increased or decreased apparent weight.

  • Apparent weight refers to the normal force (or other non-gravity forces) that produce stresses on your body tissues while trying to balance gravity.
  • When you are accelerating, the forces will not balance, meaning your apparent weight will not be the same as your actual weight (gravity).

Planetary Motion refers to the motion of planets, asteroids, comets, and other objects when orbiting around a single, central object that contains nearly all the mass of the system. The central object’s gravity is the cause of the planetary motion.

  • Kepler’s 1st Law: Objects move along elliptical orbits with the central mass (e.g. the Sun) at the focus of the ellipse. The focus is located off-centre along the major (long) axis.
  • Kepler’s 2nd Law: The areas of the “sectors” formed by the orbit and the central object will always be equal in equal amounts of time.
  • Kepler’s 3rd Law: The orbital period is related to the mean orbital radius (semi-major axis): $$T^2=kr^3$$ $$\left(\frac{T_2}{T_1}\right)^2=\left(\frac{r_2}{r_1}\right)^3$$
  • Earth’s mean orbital radius is defined as one astronomical unit (au).
  • It may be convenient to use au and years as units because, for Earth orbiting the Sun, both variables are equal to exactly 1.00.
