Centripetal Acceleration

Although the speed (scalar!) of an object is constant for uniform circular motion, the object’s velocity (vector!) is continuously changing due to changes in direction.

  • This means that the object is accelerating and there must be an unbalanced force causing the object to move in a circle.
  • If there was no unbalanced force, the object would have to move in a straight line.

The illustration below (left) shows the motion of an object from \(P_1\) to \(P_2\) along a circle centred at point C. The illustration on the right is the vector diagram showing the change in velocity (\(\Delta{\vec{\mathbf v}}\)).

  • The blue and red triangles are similar; they are both isosceles triangles with the same angles.
  • Similar triangles have the same ratio of sides:$${s \over r} = {\Delta v\over v}$$
  • Since \(s\) is the distance from \(P_1\) to \(P_2\), \(s=v\Delta t\): $${v\Delta t\over r} = {\Delta v\over v}$$
  • We can rearrange this to calculate the acceleration: $$a_c = {\Delta v\over \Delta t} = {v^2\over r}$$
  • The subscript “c” is for centripetal; it reminds us that the direction of the acceleration vector is always directed toward the centre for UCM.
  • We can get an alternate form of the equation by substituting \(v = {2\pi r\over T}\) to eliminate \(v\):$$a_c=\frac{4\pi^2r}{T^2}$$
  • These equations are valid only for the instantaneous acceleration; this is because the distance between \(P_1\) and \(P_2\) measured along the circular arc is only approximately equal to the line segment \(s\), when \(P_1\) and \(P_2\) are very close together.
  • The net force is also in the centripetal direction accoring to Newton’s 2nd Law:$${\vec{\mathbf{F}}}_{net}=m{\vec{\mathbf{a}}}_c$$

Example

A satellite orbiting at a radius of 7000 km experiences a centripetal acceleration of 8.14 m/s2. Calculate the orbital speed and period of the satellite.

$$a_c = {v^2\over r}$$

$$a_c r = v^2$$

$$v = \sqrt{a_c r}$$

$$v = \mathrm{\sqrt{8.14\ m/s^2\cdot 7.00\times 10^6\ m}}$$

$$v = \mathrm{7.55\times 10^3\ m/s}$$

$$v = {2\pi r\over T}$$

$$vT = 2\pi r$$

$$T = {2\pi r\over v}$$

$$T = \mathrm{{2\pi\cdot 7.00\times 10^6\ m\over 7.55\times 10^3\ m/s}}$$

$$T = \mathrm{5827\ s = 1.62\ h}$$
