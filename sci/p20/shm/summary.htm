Simple harmonic motion (SHM) is a type of oscillation; a back-and-forth motion centred at the object’s equilibrium.

  • SHM is caused by a restoring force that is proportional to how far the object is from its equilibium (\(\vec{\mathbf x}\)); the farther from equilibrium the stronger the net force: $$\vec{\mathbf F}_{net} = -k\vec{\mathbf x}$$
  • The potential energy associated with the restoring force is$$E_p = \frac{1}{2}kx^2$$

SHM produces a graph of position versus time that can be described mathematically by a \(\sin\) or \(\cos\) function.

  • The amplitude, \(A\), is the maximum distance from equilibrium.
  • The period of SHM is the time for one complete oscillation or cycle of the motion. $$T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{m}{k}}$$
  • The reciprocal of the period is called the frequency and is measured in hertz (1 Hz = 60 rpm). $$f = \frac{1}{T}$$
  • The maximum velocity (the steepest part of the graph) occurs when the object passes through equilibrium. $$v_{max} = \frac{2\pi A}{T}$$
  • A graph of the object’s velocity has the same shape and period as its position, but the graph is shifted one-quarter of a period.

  • The velocity and position are “out of sync” because it has the greatest speed (most \(E_k\)) when it is closest to equilibirum (least \(E_p\)).
  • A graph of the object’s acceleration is shifted another quarter of a period. $$a_{max} = \frac{v_{max}^2}{A} = \frac{4\pi^2 A}{T^2}$$

The motion of a simple pendulum is approximately SHM when the angle of the swing is less than about 30°. $$T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$

  • For larger angles the restoring force is not in direct proportion to the distance from equilibrium, due to the curved trajectory of the object.

Mechanical resonance is the tendancy of the amplitude to grow very large when an oscillator is “driven” at its harmonic or resonant frequency.

  • This happens because when the driving force is properly “synced” with the oscillations, it will always do a positive amount of work.
  • Mechanical resonance has important implications for designing buildings and other structures that can withstand earthquakes.
