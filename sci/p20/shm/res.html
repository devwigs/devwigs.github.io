Mechanical Resonance

A child on a swing behaves like a simple pendulum, with its own frequency and period given by the equation: $$T=2\pi\sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$

To push the child on a swing, you must push with the same frequency as the pendulum to ensure that you are always pushing forward and causing the child to gain energy: $$\Delta E=\vec{\mathbf{F}}\cdot\Delta\vec{\mathbf{d}}$$

  • Imagine a poorly programmed robot pushing the child at completely random times.
  • It would be just as likely that the force would be in the wrong direction, causing the child to lose energy as to gain.
  • When a force acts on a system with the same frequency as the system’s natural frequency of oscillation, scientists call this mechanical resonance.
  • The system’s natural frequency is also called the resonant frequency.

Applications

Mechanical resonance means that a force acting at a resonant frequency will add energy to the system, causing the amplitude to grow over time.

  • One important application of mechanical resonance is in designing buildings that can withstand earthquakes.
  • Buildings tend to sway with a resonant frequency.
  • When an earthquake causes the ground to shake at the building’s resonant frequency, the building is at risk of collapse.
  • Engineers can design features that can alter the resonant frequency and/or “damp” the oscillations to reduce the risk of collapse.
