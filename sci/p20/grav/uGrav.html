Physics 20

Gravitation

Sir Isaac Newton realized that the same force of gravity that causes an apple to fall from a tree can also explain the motion of planets around the Sun.

  • According to Newton’s 3rd Law, if Earth exerts a gravitaional force on the apple, the apple must exert an equal and opposite gravitational force on the Earth.
  • Gravity is a universal phenomenon: all masses produce gravity!
  • Astronomers like Johannes Kepler had studied the motion of the planets before Newton’s time.
  • Using this information, Newton concluded that the Sun’s gravity (which causes the planets’ acceleration) is: $$g = \frac{GM}{r^2}$$ where \(M\) is the mass of the Sun and \(G\) is a constant that must be measured experimentally.
  • We can combine this with \(F_g = mg\) to get: $$F_g = \frac{GMm}{r^2}$$ where \(m\) is the mass of the planet, or any other object experiencing the Sun’s gravity.

Universal Gravitation

Newton proposed that these equations apply not only to planetary orbits but are universal.

  • In other words, any two masses (no matter how small), will exert a gravitational attraction (equal and opposite) on each other... $$F_g = {Gm_1 m_2\over r^2}$$
  • This equation is known as Newton’s Law of Universal Gravitation.
  • The variable \(r\) can no longer be called the “radius” because, if Newton is correct, the equation does not require circular motion. Instead, \(r\) is called the separation between the objects.
  • This variable is always measured from the centres of the objects.

Sources & Test Masses

We can rearrange the equation: $${F_g\over m_2} = {Gm_1\over r^2}$$

  • The left side is the acceleration of \(m_2\), provided gravity is the only force!
  • The right side is the gravitational field produced by \(m_1\). $$a_2 = {F_g\over m_2} = {Gm_1\over r^2} = g_1$$
  • The gravity of \(m_1\) causes the acceleration of \(m_2\) (and vice versa.)
  • The object whose motion we are interested in is called the test mass.
  • The object(s) whose gravity affects the test mass are called the sources or field masses.

Inverse Square Law / Universal Gravitational Constant

The Law of Universal Gravitation is an “inverse square law” because, as the objects get further apart, the force gets weaker by a factor of \(r^2\).

  • In other words, when the separation between the objects doubles, the gravitational force becomes only one-quarter as strong.
  • The value of the constant \(G\) must be determined experimentally.
  • To three significant digits, the accepted value is $$G=6.67\times{10}^{-11}\mathrm{\frac{N\cdot m^2}{kg^2}}$$
  • This means that two masses of 1.00 kg each separated by a distance of 1.00 m, will attract each other with a miniscule \(6.67 \times 10^{–11}\ \mathrm{N}\) of gravitational force!

Example 1

Two spacecraft have masses of 1.20 × 106 kg and 1.80 × 106 kg. The spacecraft are 2.00 km apart. Calculate the force of gravity between the spacecraft, and their accelerations when there are no other forces present.

$$F_g=\frac{Gm_1 m_2}{r^2}$$

$$F_g= \mathrm{6.67\times 10^{-11}\ {N\cdot m^2\over kg^2}\cdot 1.20\times 10^6\ kg \cdot 1.80\times 10^6\ kg\over (2000\ m)^2}$$

$$F_g= 3.60\times 10^{-5}\ \mathrm{N}$$

$$a_1= {F_g \over m_1} = \mathrm{3.60\times 10^{-5}\ N \over 1.20\times 10^6\ kg} = \mathrm{3.00\times 10^{-11}\ m/s^2}$$

$$a_2= {F_g \over m_2} = \mathrm{3.60\times 10^{-5}\ N \over 1.80\times 10^6\ kg} = \mathrm{2.00\times 10^{-11}\ m/s^2}$$

Example 2

A meteor approaching Earth experiences an acceleration of 1.50 m/s2 due to Earth’s gravity. How far away is the meteor?

$$g=\frac{GM}{r^2}$$

$$gr^2=GM$$

$$r=\sqrt{GM\over g}$$

$$r= \mathrm{\sqrt{6.67\times 10^{-11}\ {N\cdot m^2\over kg^2}\cdot 5.97\times 10^{24}\ kg \over 1.50\ m/s^2}}$$

$$r= \mathrm{1.63\times 10^7\ m}$$

Example 3

A satellite with mass \(m_1\) orbits a planet at an orbital radius \(r_1\). A second spacecraft has double the satellite’s mass and approaches the planet at three times the separation. Calculate the ratio of the gravitational force, \(F_2\over F_1\), that the two masses experience.

$$F_1={GMm_1\over r_1^2}$$

$$F_2={GM(2m_1)\over (3r_1)^2} = {2GMm_1\over 9r_1^2}$$

$${F_2\over F_1} = {2\over 9}$$
