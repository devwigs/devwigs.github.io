  1. A student pulls a 12.8 kg wagon along a horizontal surface using an applied force of 26.2 N [25.0°]. Other forces acting on the wagon are: air resistance and other forms of friction (\({\vec{\mathbf{F}}}_f\) = 1.65 N [180°]), gravity , and the normal force of the ground pushing up on the wagon’s wheels .
    1. Draw a free body diagram (on the illustration of the wagon above) showing all forces acting on the wagon.
    2. Express all forces in Cartesian form. Fill in the table with all known quantities and show your work below.
      3. $$\vec{\mathbf F}_{ax} = \mathrm{26.2\ N \cos(25.0^\circ) = +23.7\ N}$$ $$\vec{\mathbf F}_{ay} = \mathrm{26.2\ N \sin(25.0^\circ) = +11.1\ N}$$
      \(x\)\(y\)
      \(\vec{\mathbf F}_a\)+23.7+11.1
      \(\vec{\mathbf F}_g\)0–126
      \(\vec{\mathbf F}_n\)0?
      \(\vec{\mathbf F}_f\)–1.650
      \(\vec{\mathbf F}_{net}\)?0
    3. Find the normal force by solving the \(y\)-component of Newton’s 2nd Law.
      4. The normal force must balance the other two \(y\)-forces: $$\vec{\mathbf F}_{ny} = \mathrm{0\ N - (-126 + 11.1)\ N = +114.9\ N}$$
    4. Find the acceleration by solving the \(x\)-component of Newton’s 2nd Law.
      5. $$\vec{\mathbf F}_{net} = \mathrm{+23.7\ N + (-1.65\ N) = +22.1\ N}$$ $$\vec{\mathbf a} = \frac{\vec{\mathbf F}_{net}}{m} = \mathrm{\frac{+22.1\ N}{12.8\ kg} = +1.73\ m/s^2}$$

      6. $$\begin{align} v_f &= \sqrt{v_i^2 + 2\vec{\mathbf a}\cdot\Delta\vec{\mathbf d}} \\ &= \mathrm{\sqrt{0 + 2(+1.73\ m/s^2)(+3.00\ m)}} \\ &= \mathrm{3.22\ m/s} \end{align}$$
  2. A 50.0-kilogram sign is designed to hang from two cables at 45.0° angles from the top corners as illustrated.

      2. Draw a vector diagram of the three forces. The net force must be zero because the sign is not accelerating. (The scale is 1 square = 40 newtons.)

      The easy way to find the forces (if you have taken Math 20) is to use the Law of Sines: $$F_1 = \frac{F_g}{\sin(105^\circ)} \sin(45.0^\circ) = \mathrm{359\ N}$$

      $$F_2 = \frac{F_g}{\sin(105^\circ)} \sin(30.0^\circ) = \mathrm{254\ N}$$

      Without using the Sine Law, we have to use components:

      \(x\)\(y\)
      \(\vec{\mathbf F}_g\)0–491
      \(\vec{\mathbf F}_1\)\(F_1\cos(60^\circ)\)
      \(= 0.500F_1\)      		\(F_1\sin(60^\circ)\)
      \(= 0.866F_1\)

  3. A 1.25 kg physics textbook slides from rest down a 3.00 m long ramp that is inclined at a 15.0° angle. When the book reaches the bottom of the ramp, its speed is found to be 2.42 m/s.

    1. Calculate the acceleration of the book using kinematics and use the result to calculate the net force. If you take the direction parallel to the ramp as the \(x\)-axis, you can solve the problem as a 1D uniform accelerated motion situation since there is no motion in the \(y\)-direction.
      2. $$\begin{align} \vec{\mathbf a} &= \frac{v_f^2-v_i^2}{2\Delta\vec{\mathbf d}} \\ &= \mathrm{\frac{(2.42\ m/s)^2- (0.00\ m/s)^2}{2\cdot 3.00\ m}} \\ &= \mathrm{+0.976\ m/s^2} \end{align}$$
    2. Express the force of gravity in Cartesian form. Since we are taking the \(x\)-axis as the direction parallel to the ramp, the direction of gravity is not 270° in this coordinate system.
      3. $$F_g = mg = \mathrm{1.25\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2 = 12.3\ N}$$ $$\vec{\mathbf F}_g = \mathrm{(12.3\ \cos(285^\circ), 12.3\ \sin(285^\circ))\ N} = \mathrm{(3.17, -11.8)\ N}$$
    3. Find the normal force by solving the \(y\)-component (perpendicular to the ramp) of Newton’s 2nd Law.
      4. The normal force must be +11.8 newtons since the net force is zero in the \(y\)-direction.
    4. Calculate the force of friction from the net force you obtained in (a). Use the answer to calculate the coefficient of kinetic friction.
      5. In the \(x\)-direction... $$\vec{\mathbf F}_{net} = m\vec{\mathbf a} = \mathrm{1.25\ kg\cdot (+0.976\ m/s^2) = +1.22\ N}$$ $$\vec{\mathbf F}_{net} = \vec{\mathbf F}_f + \vec{\mathbf F}_{gx}$$ $$\vec{\mathbf F}_f = \vec{\mathbf F}_{net} - \vec{\mathbf F}_{gx} = \mathrm{+1.22\ N - 3.17\ N = -1.95\ N}$$ $$\mu_k = \frac{F_f}{F_n} = \mathrm{\frac{1.95\ N}{11.8\ N} = 0.165}$$

    3. Students observe that they can incline the ramp up to a maximum angle of 18.0°. For inclinations higher than this, the object no longer remains at rest but begins to slide down the ramp. Determine the coefficient of friction between the ramp and the object. Is this coefficient the static or the dynamic friction?

      4. Repeat the above calculation changing the angle to 270 + 18.0° = 288°:

      $$\vec{\mathbf F}_g = \mathrm{(49.1\ \cos(288^\circ), 49.1\ \sin(288^\circ))\ N} = \mathrm{(15.2, -46.6)\ N}$$
      \(x\)\(y\)
      \(\vec{\mathbf F}_g\)15.2–46.6
      \(\vec{\mathbf F}_n\)0?
      \(\vec{\mathbf F}_f\)?0
      \(\vec{\mathbf F}_{net}\)00

      Since the net force must be zero, the normal force is +46.6 newtons amd the friction is –15.2 N.

      $$\mu = \frac{F_f}{F_n} = \mathrm{\frac{15.2\ N}{46.6\ N} = 0.325}$$
    4. Show that in general, the coefficient of static friction is \(\mu_s=\sin{\theta}/\cos{\theta}\) where is the incline \(\theta\) at which the mass begins to slide down the ramp.
      5. \(x\)\(y\)
      \(\vec{\mathbf F}_g\)\(mg\sin\theta\)\(-mg\cos\theta\)
      \(\vec{\mathbf F}_n\)0?
      \(\vec{\mathbf F}_f\)?0
      \(\vec{\mathbf F}_{net}\)00

      For the net force to be zero, the friction and normal forces must balance the \(x\)- and \(y\)-components of the gravitational force: $$\begin{align} \mu &= \frac{F_f}{F_n} \\ &= \mathrm{\frac{mg\sin\theta}{mg\cos\theta}} \\ &= \mathrm{\frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}} = \mathrm{\frac{\frac{Opp}{Hyp}}{\frac{Adj}{Hyp}}} \\ &= \mathrm{\frac{Opp}{Adj}} = \tan\theta \end{align}$$

  5. A pulley system is combined with an inclined plane as illustrated below and released from rest.

    1. Using \(m_1\) = 5.00 kg and \(m_2\) = 2.50 kg, \(h\) = 60.0 cm, and a ramp inclination of \(\theta\) = 10.0° from the horizontal, determine the acceleration and the speed of the system just before \(m_2\) strikes the ground. Assume the ramp is frictionless.
    2. Repeat your calculations for a coefficient of friction of \(\mu_k\) = 0.150.

      3. For a 10° incline, the direction of gravity acting on \(m_1\) is 270° – 10° = 260°. This force of gravity is...

      $$F_g = mg = \mathrm{5.00\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2 = 49.1\ N}$$ $$\vec{\mathbf F}_g = \mathrm{(49.1\ \cos(260^\circ), 49.1\ \sin(260^\circ))\ N} = \mathrm{(-8.52, -48.3)\ N}$$

      All of the forces acting on \(m_1\), and the net force, are...

      \(x\)\(y\)
      \(\vec{\mathbf F}_g\)–8.52–48.3
      \(\vec{\mathbf F}_t\)\(F_t\)0
      \(\vec{\mathbf F}_n\)0\(F_n\)
      \(\vec{\mathbf F}_f\)\(-\mu F_n\)0
      \(\vec{\mathbf F}_{net}\)\(m_1a\)0

      Because the net \(y\)-force is zero, the normal force must be +48.3 N. We can then write the \(x\)-equation as: $$\mathrm{-8.52\ N} + F_t - \mu\mathrm{(48.3\ N)} = m_1a$$

      The second mass moves moves only in the vertical direction. Newton’s 2nd Law is: $$F_t - m_2 g = -m_2a$$ becase \(m_2\) has the same acceleration as \(m_1\) but in the negative (downward) direction.

      We can combine the equations and eliminate \(F_t\) by subtracting the two equations from each other: $$\mathrm{-8.52\ N} - \mu\mathrm{(48.3\ N)} + m_2g = m_1a + m_2a$$ $$a = \frac{\mathrm{-8.52\ N} - \mu\mathrm{(48.3\ N)} + m_2g}{m_1 + m_2}$$

      When there is no friction: $$a = \mathrm{\frac{-8.52\ N + 2.50\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2}{7.50\ kg} = 2.13\ m/s^2}$$

      If \(\mu = 0.150\): $$\begin{align}a &= \mathrm{\frac{-8.52\ N - 0.150(48.3\ N)+ 2.50\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2}{7.50\ kg}} \\ &= \mathrm{1.17\ m/s^2} \end{align}$$

      We know the displacment is 0.600 metres and the initial velocity is zero, so the final velocity is: $$v_f = \sqrt{v_i^2 + 2\vec{\mathbf a}\cdot\Delta\vec{\mathbf d}} = \sqrt{2\vec{\mathbf a}\cdot\Delta\vec{\mathbf d}}$$

      Using the acceleration for zero friction gives: $$v_f = \mathrm{\sqrt{2\cdot 2.13\ m/s^2\cdot 0.600\ m} = 1.60\ m/s}$$

      Using the acceleration for \(\mu = 0.150\) gives: $$v_f = \mathrm{\sqrt{2\cdot 1.17\ m/s^2\cdot 0.600\ m} = 1.18\ m/s}$$
