Work-Energy Theorem

Reminders:

  • Forces cause objects to gain or lose kinetic energy.
  • The amount of kinetic energy gained is called the work done by the force.
  • A negative work means that kinetic energy is being lost.
  • When more than one force does work on an object, the change in kinetic energy equals the total work done by all forces.

The amount of work that a force does is determined by three factors:

  • How strong the force is.
  • The direction of the force relative to the object’s motion.
  • How far the object moves while the force is acting.

The relationship between these variables is called the work-energy theorem.

  • For 1D motion, when the force can only act in the forward or backward directions:$$W = {\vec {\mathbf F}}\cdot{\Delta\vec {\mathbf d}}$$
  • In 2D or 3D, the force can act at an angle \(\Delta\theta\) to the motion:$$W = F \Delta d \cos(\Delta\theta)$$

Example 1: A 12.0-kilogram robot uses an electric motor to propel itself with 18.0 newtons of force. If the robot moves 3.00 metres in 2.00 seconds, starting from rest...

a) How much work did the motor do?

b) Calculate the final speed of the robot if there is no friction or air resistance.

$$W_a = \Delta E_k= {\vec {\mathbf F}}\cdot{\Delta\vec {\mathbf d}} = \mathrm{18.0\ N \cdot 3.00\ m = 54.0\ J}$$

$$E_k = {1\over 2}mv^2$$

$${2 E_k\over m} = v^2$$

$$v = \sqrt{2 E_k\over m} = \mathrm{\sqrt{2\cdot 54.0\ J\over 12.0\ kg} = 3.00\ m/s}$$

c) Suppose there is a friction force of 8.00 N opposing the motion. Calculate the work done by the friction force. How would the final velocity change?

$$W_f = \mathrm{-8.00\ N\cdot 3.00\ m = -24.0\ J}$$

The total work done by both forces would be:$$\Sigma W = W_a+W_f = \mathrm{54.0\ J + (-24.0\ J) = 30.0\ J}$$

Example 2: A 20.0 kg mass accelerates from 2.00 m/s to 5.00 m/s while moving 2.00 m. Calculate the force acting on the mass.

Calculate work as the change in kinetic energy...

$$E_{ki} = {1\over 2}mv_i^2 = \mathrm{{1\over 2} 20.0\ kg\cdot (2.00\ m/s)^2 = 40.0\ J}$$

$$E_{kf} = {1\over 2}mv_f^2 = \mathrm{{1\over 2} 20.0\ kg\cdot (5.00\ m/s)^2 = 250\ J}$$

$$W = \Delta E_k = \mathrm{250\ J - 40.0\ J = 210\ J}$$

Now use the Work-Energy Theorem to find the force... $$\vec{\mathbf F} = {W\over \Delta \vec{\mathbf d}} = \mathrm{{210\ J \over 2.00\ m} = 105\ N}$$

Non-Constant Force

When a force is not constant, we must use the average force to calculate the work.

  • One way of doing this is to calculate the area under a graph of the force versus the position.

Example 3: A 100 kg cyclist experiences a force as illustrated. How much work is done and what would the cyclist’s speed be if they started from rest?

$$W = \Delta E_k = \mathrm{3000\ J}$$

$$v = \sqrt{2 E_k\over m} = \mathrm{\sqrt{2\cdot 3000\ J\over 100\ kg} = 7.75\ m/s}$$
