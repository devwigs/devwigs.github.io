  1. A 500-gram ball is dropped from a height of 2.00 metres.
    1. Calculate what the final speed of the ball should be just before it contacts the ground if the mechanical energy remains constant.

      2. The initial energy is: $$E_g = mgh = \mathrm{0.500\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 2.00\ m = 9.81\ J}$$ If no energy is added or removed, the final kinetic energy will also be 9.81 J. $$E_{kf} = \frac{1}{2} mv_f^2$$ $$\frac{2E_{kf}}{m} = v_f^2$$ $$v_f = \sqrt{\frac{2E_{kf}}{m}} = \mathrm{\sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 9.81\ J}{0.500\ kg}} = 6.26\ m/s}$$

    2. The actual final speed of the ball just before it contacts the ground is measured as 5.00 m/s. Calculate the change in mechanical energy. Draw a pair of energy bar charts and an energy flow diagram to illustrate the energy transformation.
      3. $$E_{kf} = \frac{1}{2} mv_f^2 = \mathrm{\frac{1}{2} (0.500\ kg) (5.00\ m/s)^2 = 6.25\ J}$$ The change in energy is: $$\Delta E = \mathrm{6.25\ J - 9.81\ J = -3.56\ J}$$ This means that 3.56 J energy left the system as “waste”.

      Initial Bar Chart | Final Bar Chart

    3. Does the change in mechanical energy represent energy gained by the system from the environment, or energy lost by the system to the environment?
      4. This is a loss of energy by the system to the environment.
    4. Classify the system as isolated, closed, or open. Explain.
      5. Closed. No matter enters or leaves the system but energy does.
    1. A 32.0 kg child is skating with an initial speed of 1.25 m/s. The child’s father pushes her, causing her speed to increase to 2.30 m/s. Calculate the energy added to the system by the child’s father. Draw energy bar charts to illustrate the energy transformations.
      2. $$E_{ki} =\frac{1}{2} mv_i^2 = \mathrm{\frac{1}{2} (32.0\ kg) (1.25\ m/s)^2 = 25.0\ J}$$ $$E_{kf} =\frac{1}{2} mv_f^2 = \mathrm{\frac{1}{2} (32.0\ kg) (2.30\ m/s)^2 = 84.6\ J}$$ $$\Delta E = \mathrm{84.6\ J - 25.0\ J = 59.6\ J}$$ Initial Bar Chart | Final Bar Chart

    Calculate the speed of the arrow just before it strikes the target 80.0 cm above the ground. Include appropriate energy bar charts and an energy flow diagram.

Initial Bar Chart $$E_g = mgh = \mathrm{0.0600\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot\ 1.25 m = 0.736\ J}$$ $$E_{elas} = \frac{1}{2} k(\Delta L)^2 = \mathrm{\frac{1}{2} (100\ N/m) (0.400\ m)^2 = 8.00\ J}$$ $$\Sigma E = \mathrm{8.00\ J + 0.736\ J = 8.74\ J}$$ Final Bar Chart $$\Delta E = \mathrm{-3.00\ J}$$ $$E_g = mgh = \mathrm{0.0600\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 0.800\ m = 0.471\ J}$$ $$E_k = \mathrm{8.73575\ J - 0.47088\ J - 3.00J = 5.26J}$$ $$v_f = \sqrt{\frac{2E_{kf}}{m}} = \mathrm{\sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 5.26487\ J}{0.0600\ kg}} = 13.2\ m/s}$$

  4. A 2.50 kg mass is slid up an incline (ramp) with an initial speed of 2.50 m/s (i.e. at the bottom of the ramp).
    1. Imagine that there is no friction. Use energy conservation to determine the height that the mass reaches before coming to stop and then starting to slide back down the ramp. Include energy bar charts as part of your solution.
      2. Initial: $$E_k = \frac{1}{2} mv_i^2 = \mathrm{\frac{1}{2} (2.50\ kg) (2.50\ m/s)^2 = 7.81\ J}$$ Final: $$E_g = mgh_f$$ $$h_f = \frac{E_g}{mg} = \mathrm{\frac{7.8125\ J}{2.50\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2} = 0.319\ m}$$
    2. If there is friction, would the maximum height of the mass be higher, lower, or the same as you calculated in (a)? Explain.
      3. It would be lower. Friction causes the system to lose energy as thermal and possibly sound. With less energy, the mass cannot travel as high.
    3. The maximum height that the mass reaches up the ramp is measured to be 25.0 cm. Draw a new “final” energy bar graph to determine the amount of “waste” energy produced by the friction force. Also draw an energy flow diagram.
      4. $$\Sigma E_f = mgh_f = \mathrm{2.50\ kg \cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 0.250\ m = 6.13\ J}$$ $$\Delta E = \mathrm{6.13125\ J - 7.8125\ J = -1.68\ J}$$

  5. A 625 kg roller coaster car starts from rest at a height of 15.0 metres as illustrated.

    1. Determine the speed of the car at point C, neglecting friction and air resistance. Use energy bar charts for the starting position and point C.
      2. Initial Bar Chart: $$E_g = mgh = \mathrm{625\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 15.0\ m = 91969\ J = 92.0\ kJ}$$ Point C Bar Chart: $$E_g = mgh = \mathrm{625\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 3.00\ m = 18394\ J = 18.4\ kJ}$$ Since there is no friction or air resistance to transform energy into “waste”: $$E_k = \mathrm{91969\ J - 18394\ J = 73575\ J = 73.6\ kJ}$$ $$v = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m}} = \mathrm{\sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 73575\ J}{625\ kg}} = 15.3\ m/s}$$
    2. Write a general relationship (equation) between the car’s height and speed and use it to calculate the speed at points A and B. Do this by equating the formulas for the initial and final energy rather than the calculated numerical values.
      3. $$\Sigma E_i = \Sigma E_f$$ $$mgh_i = mgh_f + \frac{1}{2} mv_f^2$$ $$gh_i = gh_f + \frac{1}{2} v_f^2$$ $$gh_i - gh_f = \frac{1}{2} v_f^2$$ $$g(h_i - h_f) = \frac{1}{2} v_f^2$$ $$2g(h_i - h_f) = v_f^2$$ $$v_f = \sqrt{2g(h_i - h_f)}$$ $$v_A = \mathrm{\sqrt{2\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot (15.0\ m - 0.00\ m)} = 17.2\ m/s}$$ $$v_B = \mathrm{\sqrt{2\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot (15.0\ m - 7.00\ m)} = 12.5\ m/s}$$
    3. When friction and air resistance are accounted for, they are responsible for transforming 21.7 kilojoules of mechanical energy into sound and/or thermal energy, between the start of the ride and point C. Recalculate the speed at point C and redraw the energy bar chart and energy flow diagram.
      4. $$\Sigma E = E_k + E_g + \Delta E$$ $$\Sigma E = \mathrm{91969\ J}$$ $$E_g = \mathrm{18394\ J}$$ $$\Delta E = W_f = \mathrm{-21700\ J}$$ $$E_k = \mathrm{91969\ J - 18394\ J - 21700\ J = 51875\ J = 51.9\ kJ}$$ $$v = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m}} = \mathrm{\sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 51875\ J}{625\ kg}} = 12.9\ m/s}$$ Bar Chart
