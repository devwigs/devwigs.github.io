Practice

  1. A baseball player hits a home run. The 250-gram baseball has a height of 9.25 metres and a speed of 24.0 m/s when it goes over the centre field wall.
    1. Calculate the baseball’s kinetic energy.
      2. $$E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \mathrm{\frac{1}{2} (0.250\ kg) (24.0\ m/s)^2 = 72.0\ J}$$
    2. Calculate the baseball’s gravitational potential energy.
      3. $$E_g = mgh = \mathrm{0.250\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 9.25\ m = 22.7\ J}$$
    3. Calculate the baseball’s mechanical energy.
      4. $$E = \mathrm{72.0\ J + 22.7 J = 94.7\ J}$$
    4. Draw an energy bar chart for the baseball (on graph paper).
      5. Bar Chart
  2. An archer has a bow with an elastic constant \(k\) = 200 N/m. She draws the bowstring back 65.0 cm.
    1. How much elastic potential energy is stored in the stretched bow?
      2. $$E_{elas} = \frac{1}{2}k(\Delta L)^2 = \mathrm{\frac{1}{2}(200\ N/m) (0.650\ m)^2 = 42.3\ J}$$
    2. If the arrow has a mass of 55.0 grams and is being held 1.60 m above the ground, how much gravitational potential energy does it have?
      3. $$E_g = mgh = \mathrm{0.0550\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2\cdot 1.60\ m = 0.863\ J}$$
    3. Calculate the mechanical energy of the bow and arrow system.
      4. $$E = \mathrm{42.25\ J + 0.863\ J = 43.1\ J}$$
    4. Draw an energy bar chart for the bow-arrow system.
      5. Bar Chart
  3. A toy car has a mass of 35.0 grams. The car is launched using a spring with \(k\) = 20.0 N/m that is compressed by 4.00 cm. Calculate the mechanical energy stored in the spring. Draw an appropriate energy bar chart.
    4. $$E_{elas} = \frac{1}{2}k(\Delta L)^2 = \mathrm{\frac{1}{2} (20.0\ N/m) (0.0400\ m)^2 = 0.0160\ J}$$ Bar Chart
    1. A 1200 kg car has 125 kJ of kinetic energy. Calculate the speed of the car.
      2. $$E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$ $$\frac{2E_k}{m} = v^2$$ $$v = \sqrt{\frac{2E_k}{m}} = \mathrm{\sqrt{\frac{2\cdot 125000\ J}{1200\ kg}} = 14.4\ m/s}$$
    2. If the car were to coast up a hill (no mechanical energy gained or lost), how high would the car reach before it begins to roll back down the hill?
      3. The mechanical energy gets transformed into gravitational potential energy: $$E_g = mgh = \mathrm{125000\ J}$$ $$h = \frac{E_g}{mg} = \mathrm{\frac{125000\ J}{1200\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2} = 10.6\ m}$$
    3. Draw energy bar charts for the car at the bottom and at the top of the hill.
      4. Bar Chart (Bottom)   Bar Chart (Top)
  5. A pinball game uses a spring that is stretched by 12.0 cm to launch a ball (\(m\) = 75.0 g) to start the game. After the spring is released, the speed of the ball is 6.00 m/s. Calculate the (a) mechanical energy of the system, and (b) elastic constant of the spring. Draw energy bar charts for the system before and after launch.

    6. After the ball is set in motion, it only has kinetic energy, so the mechanical energy is: $$E_k = \frac{1}{2} mv^2 = \mathrm{\frac{1}{2} (0.0750\ kg) (6.00\ m/s)^2 = 1.35\ J}$$ Bar Chart

    Before the ball is set in motion, the mechanical energy is in the form of elastic potential energy: $$E_{elas} = \frac{1}{2} k(\Delta L)^2$$ $$2E_{elas} = k(\Delta L)^2$$ $$k = \frac{2E_{elas}}{(\Delta L)^2} = \mathrm{\frac{2\cdot 1.35\ J}{(0.120\ m)^2} = 188\ N/m}$$ Bar Chart

  6. A solid metal ball with a mass of 200 grams rolls down a ramp starting from rest. When the ball is halfway down the ramp, its speed is measured to be 2.00 m/s. For a solid sphere that rolls without slipping, the rotational energy equation is: $$E_r=\frac{1}{5}mv^2$$
    1. Calculate the ball’s kinetic and rotational energies.
      2. $$E_k = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \mathrm{\frac{1}{2} (0.200\ kg) (2.00\ m/s)^2 = 0.400\ J}$$ $$E_{rotn} = \frac{1}{5}mv^2 = \mathrm{\frac{1}{5} (0.200\ kg) (2.00\ m/s)^2 = 0.160\ J}$$
    2. Calculate the ball’s gravitational potential energy and its mechanical energy. Remember that the ball is halfway down the ramp at this point. Assume there is no change in the mechanical energy as the ball rolls. [Before trying to answer this, draw two separate energy bar charts for the system at the (i) top and (ii) halfway point of the hill.]
      3. Because the ball is at the halfway point, it has half of the gravitational potential energy that it started with; the other half has become kinetic and rotational energy. Thus: $$E_g = E_k + E_{rotn} = \mathrm{0.400\ J + 0.160\ J = 0.560\ J}$$ $$\Sigma E = E_g + E_k + E_{rotn} = \mathrm{0.560\ J + 0.400\ J + 0.160\ J = 1.12\ J}$$
    3. Calculate the height from which the ball was released.
      4. $$E_g = mgh$$ $$h = \frac{E_g}{mg} = \mathrm{\frac{1.12\ J}{0.200\ kg\cdot 9.81\ m/s^2} = 0.571\ m}$$ Bar Chart (Animated)
