Heat Transfer

Heat flows from higher to lower temperatures. There are three main mechanisms of heat transfer:

  • Conduction
  • Convection
  • Radiation

Conduction

According to Kinetic-Molecular Theory, temperature measures the average kinetic energy of the particles.

  • When two objects at different temperatures are in contact with each other, the particles will collide with each other.
  • On average, the higher energy (hotter) particles lose energy and the lower energy (colder) particles gain energy.
  • There is a flow of heat from hot to cold.
  • This mechanism of heat transfer is called conduction.
  • Conduction occurs in solids, liquids, and gases.
  • Some materials, like most metals, are better conductors of heat.
  • Insulators, like styrofoam and fibreglass, are poor conductors of heat.

Convection

In fluids (liquids and gases), heat can be transported via convection.

  • Most fluids expand (become less dense) when heated.
  • Hotter regions will be more buoyant and will rise.
  • Colder parts of the fluid will be drawn in to replace the rising hotter fluid.
  • The net result of this effect will be a convection current.

Radiation

Objects that are really hot emit light and other forms of electromagnetic radiation.

  • Since light is a form of energy, radiation results in a decrease of temperature.
  • When the light strikes another object, that object gains energy.
  • At the temperature of the Sun (5800 K), objects radiate all colours of the rainbow.

  • At even hotter temperatures, there is more radiation produced at the violet end of the spectrum and into the untraviolet.
  • At lower temperatures, less violet light is produced and the light appears orange or red.
  • Room temperature objects emit infrared radiation; radiation like light but with a wavelength to long to be visible.

Heat & Climate

  • Convection currents in the atmosphere and the oceans play an important role in distributing heat around the Earth.

  • Radiation received from the Sun adds heat to the Earth’s climate system.
  • Radiation emitted by the Earth removes heat to the Earth’s climate system.
