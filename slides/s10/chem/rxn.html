Chemical Reactions

A chemical reaction is a rearrangement of chemical bonds to produce new substances.

  • For example when we run electricity through liquid water, it decomposes into hydrogen and oxygen gases: $$\mathrm{H_2O_{(l)} \rightarrow H_{2(g)} + O_{2(g)}}$$
  • The left side of the reaction equation describes the reactant(s).
  • The right side of the reaction equation describes the product(s).
  • The bracketed subscripts identify the phase: solid, liquid, gas, or aqueous.
  • This reaction is unbalanced because the number of oxygen atoms is different between the left and right sides.

$$\mathrm{H_2O_{(l)} \rightarrow H_{2(g)} + O_{2(g)}}$$

  • Since we need two oxygen atoms to make O2, we must start with two water molecules.

$$\mathrm{2\ H_2O_{(l)} \rightarrow H_{2(g)} + O_{2(g)}}$$

  • Now we have more hydrogen atoms on the left side (4) so we need to double the hydrogen gas.

$$\mathrm{2\ H_2O_{(l)} \rightarrow 2\ H_{2(g)} + O_{2(g)}}$$

  • This reaction equation is balanced because the number of atoms of each element is the same on both sides.

Exothermic & Endothermic

Because the products of a chemical reaction are different than the reactants, the chemical energy.

  • When the products have less chemical energy than the reactants, then energy released often takes the form of thermal energy and possibly light.
  • Reactions that release energy are called exothermic.
  • Exothermic reactions can often be identified by an increase in temperature.
  • When the products have more chemical energy than the reactants, then energy is often taken from the surroundings.
  • Reactions that absorb energy are called endothermic.
  • Endothermic reactions can often be identified by a decrease in temperature.

Formation Reactions

A formation reaction is a reaction whereby two or more elements react to form a compound.

  • Formation reactions are also called synthesis or composition reactions.

Example: Write and balance the formation reaction for ammonia.

  • Start with the unbalanced reaction:$$\mathrm{N_2 + H_2 \rightarrow NH_3}$$
  • Balance the nitrogens:$$\mathrm{N_2 + H_2 \rightarrow 2\ NH_3}$$
  • Balance the hydrogens:$$\mathrm{N_2 + 3\ H_2 \rightarrow 2\ NH_3}$$
  • Include the phases if possible:$$\mathrm{N_{2(g)} + 3\ H_{2(g)} \rightarrow 2\ NH_{3(g)}}$$

Decomposition Reactions

A decomposition reaction is the reverse of a formation reaction...

...a compound breaks down into its separate elements.

Example: Write and balance the decomposition reaction for CH3Cl.

  • Start with the unbalanced reaction:$$\mathrm{CH_3Cl \rightarrow C + H_2 + Cl_2}$$
  • Balance the hydrogen first because it will be the hardest:$$\mathrm{2\ CH_3Cl \rightarrow C + 3\ H_2 + Cl_2}$$
  • The chlorine is already balanced!
  • Do carbon last because it will be the easiest:$$\mathrm{2\ CH_3Cl \rightarrow 2\ C + 3\ H_2 + Cl_2}$$

   

Single Replacement Reactions

A single replacement reaction is when an element reacts with a compound to replace an element that is part of the compound: $$\mathrm{FeCl_3 + Ag \rightarrow Fe + AgCl}$$

  • In this unbalanced reaction, silver replaces iron in the compounds.
  • In ionic compounds, cations can only be replaced by cations and anions by anions.
  • Note that the subscript on chloride changes because the silver in has a different charge than iron(III).
  • The reaction needs to be balanced: $$\mathrm{FeCl_3 + 3\ Ag \rightarrow Fe + 3\ AgCl}$$

Example: Write and balance the SR reaction for oxygen reacting with calcium nitride.

  • Start with the unbalanced reaction:$$\mathrm{Ca_3N_2 + O_2 \rightarrow CaO + N_2}$$
  • Balance the calcium:$$\mathrm{Ca_3N_2 + O_2 \rightarrow 3\ CaO + N_2}$$
  • Balance the oxygen:$$\mathrm{Ca_3N_2 + 3\ O_2 \rightarrow 6\ CaO + N_2}$$
  • Re-balance the calcium:$$\mathrm{2\ Ca_3N_2 + 3\ O_2 \rightarrow 6\ CaO + N_2}$$
  • Balance the nitrogen:$$\mathrm{2\ Ca_3N_2 + 3\ O_2 \rightarrow 6\ CaO + 2\ N_2}$$

Double Replacement Reactions

A double replacement reaction is when two compounds react and "trade partners": $$\mathrm{FeCl_3 + Ag_2S \rightarrow Fe_2S_3 + AgCl}$$

  • The cations (\(\mathrm{Ag^+}\) and \(\mathrm{Fe^{3+}}\)) replace each other.
  • Balance the reaction:

$$\mathrm{2\ FeCl_3 + Ag_2S \rightarrow Fe_2S_3 + AgCl}$$

$$\mathrm{2\ FeCl_3 + Ag_2S \rightarrow Fe_2S_3 + 6\ AgCl}$$

$$\mathrm{2\ FeCl_3 + 3\ Ag_2S \rightarrow Fe_2S_3 + 6\ AgCl}$$

Hydrocarbon Combustion

Hydrocarbons are molecular compounds made from carbon and hydrogen, such as methane (CH4), ethane (C2H6), and propane (C3H8).

  • Hydrocarbons burn in oxygen to produce water and carbon dioxide: $$\mathrm{C_3H_8 + O_2 \rightarrow H_2O + CO_2}$$
  • Reactions like this are called hydrocarbon combustion.
  • Sometimes carbon monoxide can be produced instead of, or in addition to carbon dioxide. This is an incomplete combustion.
  • Hydrocarbon combustion is an exothermic reaction that releases heat and light.
  • Balance carbon first, then hydrogen, and oxygen last.

    • $$\mathrm{C_3H_8 + O_2 \rightarrow H_2O + CO_2}$$

    $$\mathrm{C_3H_8 + O_2 \rightarrow H_2O + 3\ CO_2}$$

    $$\mathrm{C_3H_8 + O_2 \rightarrow 4\ H_2O + 3\ CO_2}$$

    $$\mathrm{C_3H_8 + 5\ O_2 \rightarrow 4\ H_2O + 3\ CO_2}$$
